## 1. The contest as posed

The Sprint 2 architectural panel (28 April 2026, §3.viii) set `notes.cross_cultural_variance: high` for six aesthetic-affective latents — L25 (Coziness), L32 (Prestige Cues), L34 (Welcome), L36 (Awe), L39 (Formality), L40 (Playfulness) — on the panel's best judgement, while flagging the assignment as a preserved contest because Keltner & Haidt (2003) provide a credible counter-argument, particularly for L36. The Sprint 2 disposition explicitly invited the next cohort to "audit this list, with particular attention to Keltner & Haidt's (2003) cross-cultural awe research as a counter-argument to the L36 `high` flag" (§5, Contest 3).

This document resolves the contest for L25, L36, and L40. The other three flagged latents (L32, L34, L39) are not adjudicated here and remain at `high`.

## 2. Three position statements

**Henrich, Heine, & Norenzayan — the WEIRD-default position.** The aesthetic-affective literature is dominated by samples drawn from Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich, and Democratic societies, which represent a tiny and non-representative slice of human psychological variation (Henrich, Heine, & Norenzayan, 2010). For any affective construct whose operationalisation rests on WEIRD-sample norms, the default assumption must be `high` cross-cultural variance — the burden of proof falls on the universalist, not on the particularist. Apparent universals reported from two- or three-culture comparisons (typically WEIRD, East Asian, and one other) systematically underestimate the variance that emerges when the sampling frame extends to small-scale, non-industrial, or non-literate societies. Default to `high` unless the construct has been validated across at least a dozen cultures spanning multiple subsistence and political ecologies.

**Keltner & Haidt — the cross-cultural-universal position for awe.** Awe has two core appraisal components — perceived vastness and a need for cognitive accommodation — that recur across cultural and historical contexts (Keltner & Haidt, 2003). Subsequent multi-country work supports the position: Yaden et al. (2019) document awe-elicitation across 26 countries and find substantial structural agreement on the elicitors and the phenomenology, even where the meaning ascribed to the experience and its downstream behavioural sequelae diverge sharply. The cultural variance is real but located — it concerns *meaning* and *consequence*, not the perception itself. For a registry concerned with image-extractable affective response to scenes (vast natural settings, towering architecture, cosmic phenomena), the appraisal-level component is the relevant signal, and at the appraisal level the variance is `medium`, not `high`.

**Markus & Kitayama — the self-construal-moderation position.** Independent vs. interdependent self-construal moderates many affective constructs, particularly those that are *socially* embedded (Markus & Kitayama, 1991). The moderation is strongest for affects whose meaning depends on relational positioning — welcome (L34), formality (L39), prestige (L32) — and weaker for affects whose elicitors are perceptual or environmental rather than social. Coziness (L25) sits in an intermediate position: its elicitors are partly perceptual (warm light, enclosure, soft texture) but its connotation is heavily socially loaded (the gathering, the home, the company). Playfulness (L40) similarly straddles perceptual and social loadings, with the social side dominating in adult contexts. For both, `high` is defensible.

## 3. Per-latent vote

**L25 Coziness/Warmth — KEEP `high`.** The non-translatability of the cluster is decisive: Danish *hygge*, German *gemütlichkeit*, Spanish *convivencia*, Italian *nido*, Dutch *gezelligheid*, and Japanese *aitsugu* are not synonyms — they connote subtly different affective registers, different relational presuppositions, and different prototypical settings. The English label "coziness" cannot serve as a culture-neutral descriptor, and the WEIRD-sample dominance (Henrich et al., 2010) of the literature means that the construct as currently operationalised reflects a specifically Northern-European-and-derivative aesthetic. Markus & Kitayama's self-construal moderation reinforces the case: coziness is a relational-affective construct, and its meaning shifts with the cultural framing of the relationship between self and intimate-other. Vote: `high`. Henrich and Markus concur; Keltner abstains (no awe-relevant contention).

**L36 Awe/Grandeur — DOWNGRADE to `medium`.** Keltner & Haidt's (2003) two core appraisals — vastness and need for accommodation — replicate across the cultures studied to date, and the multi-country evidence in Yaden et al. (2019) shows structural agreement on awe-elicitors at the appraisal level. The image-extractable signal that the registry's L36 detector is meant to capture is the appraisal-level perception, not the downstream meaning or behavioural sequel. At the appraisal level the variance is real but moderate: cultural inflection of *what counts* as vast or cognitively-overwhelming exists, but the perception of vastness in (say) a cathedral interior, an alpine vista, or a starfield is broadly stable. Henrich & Norenzayan's WEIRD critique is acknowledged but does not, in this case, push the assignment to `high`: the awe literature is one of the better-sampled corners of affective science, and the cross-cultural evidence is genuinely substantial. Vote: `medium`. Keltner leads; Henrich concedes that awe is one of the cases where the WEIRD default is overridden by evidence; Markus notes the meaning-vs-perception distinction supports the downgrade.

**L40 Playfulness — KEEP `high`.** Play norms vary substantially across cultures: the form, content, and acceptable contexts of children's play differ across cultural ecologies (Roopnarine, Patte, Johnson, & Kuschner, 2014), and adult play is even more culturally variable, ranging from highly ritualised forms (Japanese *asobi*, Balinese cock-fighting in Geertz's classic ethnography) to improvisational and informal expressions characteristic of contemporary Western settings. Markus & Kitayama's self-construal moderation applies forcefully here: playfulness in independent-self cultures licenses individual self-expression and mild rule-breaking, while in interdependent-self cultures it more often takes the form of role-aligned group activity. The image-extractable signal — toys, games, informal posture, bright colour, smile expression — is not culture-neutral; the registry's L40 detector trained on WEIRD imagery will systematically misread non-WEIRD play scenes. Vote: `high`. Henrich and Markus concur; Keltner concurs (the awe-style universalist argument does not extend to play, where the ethnographic record is unambiguous).
