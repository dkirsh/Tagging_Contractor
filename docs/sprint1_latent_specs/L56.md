## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Support for standing, circulating socializing in residual floor space and at perch surfaces.

### definition_long
The degree to which a setting affords brief, mobile, standing-and-circulating sociability rather than seated, focused encounter — operationalised through the joint presence of (a) open floor pockets that admit small standing clusters without obstructing through-movement, (b) bar-height or leaning surfaces ("perches") that anchor a drink, plate, or elbow without committing the body to a chair, and (c) circulation paths legible enough that newcomers can enter, drift, and exit a cluster without social cost (Whyte, 1980, pp. 16–25). The construct stands in the lineage of Whyte's plaza studies, Oldenburg's (1989) third-place criterion of "the regulars" sustained by easy come-and-go, and Hillier and Hanson's (1984) configurational claim that mingling is a property of how a room sits in a wider movement system, not of the room considered in isolation. As tagged here, L56 is a *local* affordance score: it asks whether the room *could* host mingling were people to be drawn through it, not whether they in fact are. The configurational question — whether the room sits on an integrated axial line and is therefore actually frequented — is held in `extractability.configurational_measure: integration` and flagged for Sprint 2 split (see `scope_excludes`).

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A hotel lobby with a long bar-height ledge along one wall, two free-standing high tables in the centre, and a clear 1.5 m-wide loop around them — three or four standing pairs could form without blocking the door to the breakfast room.
2. A conference foyer between sessions: round high tables scattered three to five metres apart, no fixed seating in the central zone, coffee station offset to one side so queues do not cross the standing clusters.
3. A craft brewery taproom with a window-counter perch facing the street, a central pour-bar, and a generous residual floor that lets parties of two to four orbit the bar and re-cluster without colliding.
4. An art-gallery atrium during an opening: paintings on the perimeter walls, a central catering table, and a clear annular floor between them on which standing groups form, dissolve, and reform around the wine.
5. A university department's "milk-and-biscuits" common room with a tall bench along one wall, a kitchenette counter at standing height, and a deliberately under-furnished centre — the room is empty most of the day and full at 11 a.m.
6. A street-front café whose front quarter has been left as standing-only counter space facing the window, with the seated tables relegated to the rear; commuters lean, talk, and rotate through.
7. A market hall whose stalls are arranged with wide cross-aisles and intermittent leaning rails, so visitors pause, talk, and continue without the spatial commitment of sitting down.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A formal banqueting hall packed with round seated tables — sociable, but seated and committed; the geometry forecloses the easy entry-and-exit that defines mingling.
2. A narrow corridor used opportunistically for chats: people *do* mingle there, but the geometry punishes it (blocking through-movement), so the room scores low despite the observed behavior.
3. A waiting room with rows of fixed bench seating facing a screen — co-presence without standing affordance, and explicitly oriented away from mutual engagement.
4. A wide-open empty warehouse with no perches, no high tables, and no anchoring surfaces — large *floor*, but nothing for the body to lean against, so groups do not self-organise; the affordance is for movement, not mingling.
5. A library reading room — open floor exists between the shelves but the social contract forbids standing conversation; the geometry permits mingling, the institutional frame prohibits it (this is correctly L56-low because the construct is about *the room's contribution to the affordance*, not the institutional layer).
6. A nightclub dance floor — mobile, social, and standing, but oriented around music-and-dance rather than conversational clustering; the affordance is a different one (peripheral_engagement / shared-focus), and L56 should not absorb it.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Settings whose seated furniture is *deliberately* sparse to leave room for standing clusters (the "under-furnished centre" pattern — counts as positive evidence).
- Settings where perches are distributed (≥ 2 distinct standing-anchor surfaces visible in the image) rather than concentrated at a single bar — distribution is part of the construct.
- Mixed-mode rooms in which a seated zone and a mingling zone coexist, scored on the mingling zone's affordance not on a room average.
- Outdoor and semi-outdoor plaza-style settings (Whyte's original substrate) — the construct is not interior-only.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- The *configurational* question of whether the room is actually frequented (i.e., sits on an integrated axial line in the wider plan). This is held in `configurational_measure: integration` and flagged for a Sprint-2 split into L56a (local pocket geometry) and L56b (configurational frequentation). Hillier's argument that L56 fuses two genuinely distinct properties is acknowledged here; the Sprint 1 compromise is to keep L56 unitary but to mark the conflation explicitly so downstream consumers can model it.
- Standing-and-moving settings whose primary social organisation is *focused* (dance floor, sports rink, performance pit) — these belong to other constructs.
- Pure circulation infrastructure (corridors, foyers used only for transit) — the *opportunistic* mingling that occurs there does not establish the affordance; the geometry must invite the pause.
- Seated sociability, however vibrant — the construct is constitutively about the *standing-and-circulating* mode, not sociability in general.

### extraction_notes_2d
The extractor combines three streams against image-only inputs. First, upstream tag `furniture.standing_surface_count` (expected name; Phase D may rename) enumerates bar-height tables, leaning rails, counters, and ledges; a count ≥ 2 with spatial dispersion ≥ a threshold contributes positively. Second, upstream tag `affordance.free_floor_area_fraction` (expected) estimates the proportion of the visible floor unoccupied by furniture or fixed obstacles; a mid-to-high range (roughly 0.35–0.65) contributes positively, while extremes contribute negatively (a packed room cannot host clusters; a vast empty floor offers no anchoring). Third, upstream tag `affordance.circulation_path_clearance` (expected) confirms that through-movement of approximately person-width remains possible around the standing zones. The composite is calibrated on Western interior corpora and should be flagged for cultural variance at the medium level — settings such as Japanese standing-bars (`tachinomi`) and Mediterranean standing-coffee bars use markedly different perch and spacing conventions and may be systematically under- or over-scored by a Western-trained extractor (Hall, 1966). Where floor-plan augmentation is available, integration value from the axial map enters as a multiplicative gate: a high local pocket score in a globally segregated room (low integration) is downweighted, since Hillier and Hanson (1984) show such rooms remain unfrequented regardless of local affordance. In purely image-only mode the integration term is omitted and the construct is scored as a local-affordance ceiling.

### extraction_notes_3d
A 3D / VR or monocular-depth pipeline strengthens every component. Free-floor-area becomes a metric quantity from the depth map (square metres of unobstructed standing surface within the bounded room), with a target band of roughly 4–15 m² of *residual* floor for a small mingling room and proportionally more for larger ones. Standing surfaces are confirmed at the canonical perch height range (1.0–1.2 m) rather than inferred from class label alone, distinguishing true bar-height tables from misclassified high stools or planter rims. Distributed-perch geometry can be tested directly: the centroids of standing surfaces should not all lie on a single axis (which would indicate a single bar, not distribution) but should populate at least two non-collinear cluster sites separated by ≥ 2 m. Circulation paths are validated by tracing a 0.7 m-clearance corridor between cluster sites and to room exits. With a floor plan, the room's integration value is computed against the wider axial map and applied as the configurational gate described above; without it, depth-derived `affordance.local_isovist_area` from the room's interior nodes provides a within-room proxy. The 3D version of the extractor should still emit a Hall-zone distance estimate for the *expected* inter-cluster spacing, since cluster geometry is itself culturally indexed.

### Iteration 1
*"Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?"*

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: The examples — hotel lobby with bar-height ledge and high tables, conference foyer with scattered standing tables, brewery taproom with a window-counter, gallery opening with annular standing space, departmental milk-and-biscuits room, café standing-counter, market hall — converge on settings that *invite people to stand, anchor lightly to a surface, and orbit in small clusters that form, dissolve, and reform*.
