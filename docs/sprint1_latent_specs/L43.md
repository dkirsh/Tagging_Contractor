## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Degree to which entry geometry and seating frontage invite a stranger to initiate contact.

### definition_long
Approach Invitation indexes the extent to which a setting's entry geometry, frontage, and barrier configuration jointly *invite* an unacquainted person — or a group already co-present — to initiate light contact, take a seat near others, or move into the social field of those present. The construct is grounded in Whyte's (1980) observations that "what attracts people most, in sum, is other people" but only when the physical envelope removes the small frictions that otherwise deter approach: turnstiles, walls of backs, raised stages, ambiguous thresholds, missing edges to "perch" on (Whyte, 1980, pp. 19–22). Oldenburg (1989) extends the same logic to "third places," where low approach friction is the constitutive affordance distinguishing a tavern or café from a private club. The construct is *unfocused* in Goffman's (1963) sense — it concerns the conditions of co-presence, not of sustained mutual involvement — and is keyed to the moment *prior to* any focused encounter beginning.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A coffee-shop window counter where the row of stools faces outward through a glass front and the doorway sits flush with the sidewalk; a passer-by can see seated patrons and step in without crossing a threshold zone.
2. A park bench placed perpendicular to a walking path with a clear sightline to the path and no planter or railing intervening, such that a walker may sit at one end without having to step around a barrier.
3. A library reading-room entrance where the first table sits within four metres of the door, chairs angle toward the entry, and there is no service desk forming a queue-fence between the door and the seats.
4. A campus quad with low retaining walls at sittable height running along the main diagonal, oriented so seated students face the path; new arrivals can perch beside a seated person without having to "intrude."
5. A restaurant patio with seating that faces the sidewalk through a low rail (not a hedge or a wall), and a clearly marked entry that opens directly onto the seating area rather than a host stand.
6. A community-centre lounge whose entrance opens onto an arc of armchairs facing the door, with no reception counter or rope line between the doorway and the chairs.
7. A market stall where the produce table extends to the aisle edge and the vendor stands behind facing outward, allowing browsers to drift up without crossing a perceived "shop interior" boundary.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A hotel lobby with deep plush seating arranged in a circle facing inward around a central coffee table — visually warm, but the seated group's backs form a barrier to any newcomer, and the chairs are too low to perch on. (Looks inviting; is closed.)
2. A trendy café where the entrance opens onto a long communal table parallel to the door — seats are technically available, but every patron sits with their side to the door, no faceable edge, and the host stand interposes. (Open seating, no approach invitation.)
3. A park with abundant lawn but no edges, walls, or benches; people may walk through but there is no perch from which one can be approached or approach. (High permeability, zero invitation.)
4. A gallery atrium with a single curved bench in the centre of a polished floor, facing a wall — sittable, but isolated, with no orientation toward circulation; the seated person is *exposed* but not *invitable*.
5. An outdoor plaza with benches that face away from the main pedestrian flow toward a parking lot — backs to the action, sociofugal in effect even if the chairs are technically present.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Settings where the entry threshold is geometrically continuous with the social interior (no foyer, no host stand, no queue-fence).
- Settings where seating frontage faces the path of approach rather than away from it.
- Settings where edges (low walls, wide sills, planter caps) are sittable at hip-to-knee height and run parallel to circulation.
- The case of approach by a *single* person (Goffman, 1971, *Relations in Public*, pp. 19–27).

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- The *with/single distinction* (Goffman, 1971, pp. 19–27): a bench or edge invites a single very differently than it invites a "with" (a couple, family, or already-conversing pair). This V2.6 cue list elides the distinction; L43 measures invitation to approach the setting *as such*, not the differential invitation extended to a single versus a with. A future L43b should split.
- The *quality of conversation* once approach has occurred — that is L44 (Sociopetal Seating Topology) and L48–L49 (F-formation arrangement).
- The mere availability of seats with no consideration of orientation or barrier configuration — that is closer to L56 (Mingling Affordance).
- Cases where invitation is *role-mediated* (a greeter, a host, a doorman) rather than geometrically afforded — that is L46 (Hosting/Service Script).
- Approach by an already-acquainted party returning to a known group (this is rejoining, not approaching).

### extraction_notes_2d
Extraction relies on four upstream observable families, each a distinct Phase D contract. (i) Entry geometry: upstream tags `affordance.threshold_count`, `affordance.entry_to_first_seat_distance_m`, and `affordance.host_barrier_present` parse the doorway-to-seat path and detect interposed reception counters or queue rails; this is the dominant L43 signal and the primary feature distinguishing L43 from sibling L44. (ii) Faceable-edge inventory: upstream tag `affordance.sittable_edge_length_m` (perimeter of low walls, planters, sills at hip-to-knee height) and `affordance.edge_orientation_to_circulation` (parallel vs. perpendicular vs. away). (iii) Frontage orientation: upstream tag `affordance.seating_front_vector_to_path_alignment` (the cosine between mean chair/bench front-vector and the dominant pedestrian axis). (iv) Barrier inventory: upstream tag `affordance.barrier_count_at_approach_axis` (planters, rails, hedges, ropes intersecting the entry-to-seat path). The composite is robust to occlusion of individual seats because the entry-geometry signal carries most of the variance. **Western-calibration warning (Hall, 1966; cross_cultural_variance: high):** the construct as scored here is calibrated against North-European/North-American norms in which face-to-face frontal approach at personal-far distance is the canonical "invitation." Mediterranean settings tolerate closer angles; Japanese settings often substitute side-by-side around a shared focus, in which case the same image will read as low-invitation despite being high-invitation in its own cultural register. Downstream consumers MUST treat the L43 score as culturally indexed. **With/single elision warning (Goffman, 1971, pp. 19–27):** the score does not distinguish invitation extended to a single person from invitation extended to a "with"; a bench that warmly invites a single may actively deter a couple, and vice versa. Phase F.5 should evaluate whether to split L43 into L43a (single-approach) and L43b (with-approach).

### extraction_notes_3d
In 3D / VR substrates, the same four upstream families are computed from richer geometric primitives. (i) Entry geometry: full 3D doorway-to-seat path-length and visibility-to-first-seat solid-angle from the entry point, plus host-stand bounding-box detection in the entry corridor. (ii) Faceable-edge inventory: 3D ledge segmentation gives true sittable-edge length at hip-to-knee height (rather than projected length from a single image) and supports the `edge_orientation_to_circulation` measure as a 3D vector dot product. (iii) Frontage orientation: chair/bench front-vectors are recovered directly from object-pose estimation rather than 2D heuristics, giving substantially less noise on the cosine-alignment measure. (iv) Barrier inventory: 3D occluder segmentation (planters, rails, hedges) is straightforward; the approach-axis from any candidate entry point can be queried and intersected against the occluder set. In VR walk-throughs, an additional behavioural proxy is available: time-to-first-seat for a naive avatar approach, which gates the geometric score. None of these 3D extensions changes the construct meaning, only the noise floor of the upstream observables.

### bn_notes
The Spohn 50%-overlap rule is the dominant Engineer-pass concern here. L44 (Sociopetal Seating Topology) shares the chair-orientation observable family with L43, and a naive parent-tag list for both would overlap heavily on `affordance.seating_front_vector_*` family members. To honour the 50% rule, L43's `parent_tags` deliberately *omits* chair-orientation tags as direct parents and instead relies on (a) entry geometry (`threshold_count`, `entry_to_first_seat_distance_m`, `host_barrier_present`), (b) sittable-edge inventory (`sittable_edge_length_m`, `edge_orientation_to_circulation`), and (c) the approach-axis barrier inventory (`barrier_count_at_approach_axis`). The frontage-orientation signal enters the L43 extractor only through the `edge_orientation_to_circulation` tag, which is a circulation-axis-relative measurement, not the chair-pair-relative measurement that L44 will use. Phase D `audit-extraction-plan` should verify zero overlap on chair-pair-relative tags and at most one shared tag on edge tags.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
*"Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?"*

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: Reading only the seven positive and five negative examples without consulting the definition: the positives all share a feature pattern of (i) an entry that opens onto seating without a host-stand or queue-rail interposed, (ii) seating or sittable edges oriented toward the path of approach (faceable), and (iii) the absence of barriers (planters, rails, walls of backs) along the line from entry to seat. The negatives violate at least one of these three: lobby with backs-as-barrier; communal table with no faceable edge; lawn with no perch; isolated bench with no path orientation; benches facing parking lot. The inferred construct is something like "geometric invitation to enter and sit near present others, scored on the entry-frontage-barrier triad."

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: Converged. The Pass 1 definition_long names "entry geometry, frontage, and barrier configuration" as the constitutive triad and the blind read recovered exactly this triad from the examples without prompting. The unfocused-mode framing also recovers naturally — none of the examples features sustained mutual involvement; all are about the conditions of the moment *before* any encounter begins.
