## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
How well the space scaffolds orderly, single-file service interactions and turn order.

### definition_long
The degree to which the spatial setting provides legible, orderly scaffolding for sequential service encounters — that is, the extent to which a setting publishes "who is next" through stanchions, floor markings, a defined waiting line, and a single point of service such that users can reliably take turns without verbal negotiation. The construct is rooted in Mann's (1969) classic field study of queue behaviour, in which physical regimentation reduced ambiguity about precedence and lowered conflict at service points; in Larson's (1987) analysis showing that perceived waiting time is mediated as much by queue *legibility* as by queue *length*; and in Goffman's (1963, pp. 23–24) treatment of "withs" and "singles" who must reconcile their bodily orientation to the published order of service. L47 measures the *spatial* contribution to that scaffolding — the extent to which the room itself answers the question "where do I stand and who is next?" — independently of whether a queue is presently in use.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A bank lobby with retractable belt stanchions zig-zagging between roped-off lanes, terminating at a single "next teller" sign and a numerical now-serving display.
2. A coffee-shop counter with a printed floor decal reading "ORDER HERE" at one end and "PICK UP HERE" at the other, with a painted line on the floor connecting the entry point to the order point.
3. A pharmacy consultation window with a clearly marked privacy line two metres back from the counter, a "wait here" floor sticker, and a single service window with a "Next Customer" backlit sign.
4. A passport-control hall with permanent metal railings forming a serpentine queue, overhead "next available officer" lights, and a single embarkation point at each booth.
5. A deli counter with a paper take-a-number dispenser at the entry, a digital "now serving 47" sign above the cases, and a defined customer side bounded by a low rail.
6. An airline check-in area with floor-mounted stanchion bases and posted lane discipline (Premium / Economy), each lane single-file and terminating at one agent at a time.
7. A theme-park ride entrance with a long switchback queue defined by metal pipe railings, regular "wait time from this point" signage, and a single turnstile at the boarding platform.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A crowded farmers'-market stall where customers cluster in a semicircle around the produce, calling out orders as the vendor catches their eye — high service activity, zero queue scaffolding (this is opportunistic mingling, not queue support).
2. A hotel concierge desk with two staff but no rope or floor marking, where guests form ad-hoc clusters and rely entirely on verbal "are you in line?" negotiation — service exists, queue *infrastructure* does not.
3. A restaurant host stand with a clipboard and a verbal waitlist but no spatial queue and no posted next-position cue — turn-taking is sociotemporal but not spatially scaffolded.
4. An empty corridor with stanchions stored against the wall and a "queue here when busy" sign — the materials exist but the *configuration* does not currently scaffold queueing (queue infrastructure is latent rather than deployed).
5. A self-checkout area with six kiosks and a single roving attendant, where customers approach whichever machine frees up first without any single-point-of-service line — this is a parallel-service topology, not a queue.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Settings where a queue scaffold is *deployed* even if no one is currently waiting (the configuration is what is being measured, not the live behaviour).
- "Virtual" queue scaffolding via take-a-number systems plus ample seating, when paired with a clear single point of call (as in DMV waiting rooms).
- Multi-lane queues where each lane is itself single-file and terminates at one server — the construct measures *per-lane* turn-taking scaffolding.
- Hybrid hosted-and-queued settings (e.g., a restaurant podium with both a posted waitlist and a defined waiting bench).

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- *Service script clarity* (L46): whether one understands what to *do* at the counter once one's turn comes is a different construct from whether the *path to the counter* is legibly ordered. L47 ends at the service point; L46 begins there.
- Crowd-density management without any turn-order claim (e.g., a museum with directional flow markings to manage congestion but no service encounter at all).
- Queue *behaviour* (whether people are actually in line, in order, and not cutting). L47 is the *spatial affordance* for orderly turn-taking; the behaviour is downstream and culturally moderated.
- Self-service or parallel-service topologies (food-court ordering kiosks, supermarket self-checkout banks) where there is no single-server termination.
- Egress queueing (theatre exits, bus boarding lines) — those are throughput-management settings; L47 specifically targets *service* encounters.

### value_type assignment

The 0–4 Likert is interpreted as: 0 = no queue scaffolding visible; 1 = ambiguous or partial cues (a single floor decal, no other support); 2 = some scaffolding (one of {stanchions, single point of service, next-position cue} present); 3 = most scaffolding present (two of three); 4 = full scaffolding (defined line + single-point service + clear next-position cue, ideally with redundancy across modalities).

### extraction_notes_2d
Extraction proceeds via VLM prompted with the V2.6 indicator set. The VLM is given the image and asked to score, on the 0–4 Likert above, whether each of three sub-cues is present: (a) a defined waiting line, evidenced by upstream tag `affordance.stanchion_count` ≥ 1 OR `affordance.floor_marking_present` = true OR a continuous railing flagged by upstream segmentation under class `barrier_railing`; (b) a single point of service, evidenced by upstream tag `furniture.counter_count` = 1 within the local region OR a single staffed `service_window` detection; (c) a clear next-position cue, evidenced by upstream `signage.numeric_display_present`, `signage.next_customer_marker`, or a "wait here" floor decal under `floor_marking.text_present`. The VLM aggregates the three sub-cues into the 0–4 score using the rubric in `value_type assignment`. **Critical 2D limitation:** static images cannot resolve whether the stanchions are *deployed* in service of an active queue topology or merely stored along a wall awaiting deployment; the prompt must instruct the VLM to penalise apparent storage configurations. **Cultural note:** queue-support cues are calibrated against North American and North-European hosting norms (Mann, 1969; Larson, 1987); settings in cultures with different queueing conventions (e.g., contexts where simultaneous-approach service is normative) may score artificially low — flag this in extractor output but do not adjust the score.

### extraction_notes_3d
In 3D / VR substrates, extraction is straightforward and approaches `yes` confidence: stanchion meshes, floor decals, and service-window geometry are all directly recoverable as scene-graph entities, and the spatial relation between the line endpoint and the service point can be computed as a path through the navigable region. The VLM is replaced or augmented by a geometric pass that (i) identifies all `barrier_stanchion` and `barrier_railing` instances in the scene, (ii) traces their topology to determine whether they form a continuous lane terminating near (iii) a single `service_counter` or `service_window` instance, and (iv) checks for `signage.next_customer_marker` instances within sight of the lane endpoint. The 0–4 score is computed deterministically from the geometric primitives. Floor-plan augmentation is not required because the construct is local to a single room or service area. Monocular-3D-approx is `partial` because the depth and topology of stanchion lanes are recoverable in principle but stanchion-to-stanchion connectivity is fragile under occlusion.

## Notes

### interaction_mode
focused — turn-taking presupposes a sustained mutual involvement around the service encounter, even if the user's involvement with co-queuers is limited to civil inattention. The shared concern is the orderly transition through the service point.

### cross_cultural_variance
medium — Mann (1969) and Larson (1987) document robust queueing norms in North American and North-European service settings, and similar physical scaffolds (stanchions, take-a-number) recur across most industrialised contexts. However, the *rigour* with which spatial scaffolding is honoured varies (e.g., simultaneous-approach service is normatively acceptable in some Mediterranean and South Asian contexts, in which a low-L47 score reflects a different cultural norm rather than poor design). The flag is set to `medium` rather than `high` because the *infrastructure cues* themselves (stanchions, floor decals, single counter) are visually consistent across cultures even where adherence patterns differ; a future Sprint 2 audit may revisit this if cross-cultural evaluation data warrants.

### extraction_notes
No V2.6 Tier override. V2.6 Tier 2 → registry mapping (from_2d: `partial`, from_3d_vr: `yes`, monocular_3d_approx: `partial`) is honoured. The Tier 2 placement reflects the briefing's special note that queue *infrastructure* is recoverable from a 2D image but queue *function* requires behavioural evidence the image cannot supply; the `partial` rating on `from_2d` encodes exactly this limitation. The VLM-based `method_family` is appropriate because the construct is best read by a model that can integrate signage text, floor markings, barrier topology, and service-point identification into a single ordinal judgment; no clean geometric primitive captures the conjunction.
