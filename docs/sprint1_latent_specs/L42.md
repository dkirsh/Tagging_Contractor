## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Mutual awareness — the spatial opportunity to see others and to be seen.

### definition_long
Interactional Visibility is the degree to which a setting offers occupants the opportunity to perceive, and be perceived by, other co-present persons across the bounded region of the setting. The construct names a configurational property of space — a function of sightlines, partition heights, the placement and transparency of boundaries, and the depth of view a vantage permits — independent of whether anyone is currently looking. Following Hillier and Hanson (1984), the natural operationalisation is the isovist (the polygon of points visible from a given vantage), whose area, perimeter, and connectivity to neighbouring isovists summarise how exposed and how exposing a position is. Whyte (1980) showed empirically that public spaces with reciprocal sightlines produce higher levels of triangulation and informal contact than spaces of equal area but lower mutual visibility. The construct is to be read in the unfocused-interaction register (Goffman, 1963): it measures the *opportunity structure* for co-awareness, not the substance of any particular encounter that opportunity might enable.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. An open-plan academic library reading room with knee-height carrels and unbroken sightlines from the entry to the far wall, where every reader can see and be seen by every other reader.
2. A glass-walled conference room set within an open office, such that the room's occupants and the office's occupants are mutually visible across the transparent boundary.
3. A plaza ringed by café tables on a slight slope, where seated patrons can survey the whole plaza and are themselves visible to anyone standing within it.
4. A hospital nurses' station designed as an island in the centre of an open ward, with low counters and no visual obstructions to patient bays.
5. A school cafeteria with long communal tables and no booth partitions, where children at any seat can see children at any other seat and the supervising adults at the periphery.
6. A shopping-mall food court with low-back seating and a balcony overlook, where ground-floor diners and balcony-level passers-by are mutually within view.
7. A community-centre lobby with floor-to-ceiling glass on three sides, exposing the interior to the street and the street to the interior.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A library with the same nominal floor area but with shoulder-height stack ranges arranged in a comb pattern, so that any given reader can see only the few readers in the same aisle and is invisible to everyone else — sightlines are short and the isovist of any vantage is small.
2. A traditional cubicle farm in which every workstation is enclosed by 1.6 m partitions on three sides; the floor is *occupied* by many people but mutual visibility is near-zero except across narrow corridor crossings.
3. A restaurant with high-back booth seating and a curtained partition between sections — diners are co-present in the building but the booth geometry forecloses cross-table sightlines.
4. A reading nook within a larger library — comfortable, with windows to the outside, but situated at the end of a stack run such that the nook's occupant cannot see the rest of the library and is not seen from it. (Outdoor visibility through a window does not count: the construct is mutual visibility *among co-present occupants of the setting*.)
5. A modern museum gallery with deliberately staged "discovery" alcoves: each alcove contains a single artefact and a single viewer at a time, and the alcove walls block the viewer from being seen by, or seeing, viewers in adjacent alcoves.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Reciprocal-visibility configurations across transparent or low-partition boundaries (glass walls, half-height screens) where the boundary is physical but not visual.
- Outdoor-to-indoor visibility *between co-present social regions* (e.g., an open café terrace whose patrons are visible to and from the indoor seating area), provided both regions are part of the same bounded social setting.
- Vantage-dependent visibility variation: the construct is properly understood as a *field* over the setting (visibility differs by vantage); the registry value summarises the field, typically by isovist-area-weighted mean.
- Visibility through low or partial obstructions (planters, balustrades, half-walls) that screen bodies but not heads.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- *Quality of mutual visibility for civil-inattention purposes.* Goffman (1963, pp. 83–88) argued that civil inattention requires the *opportunity to disattend without giving offense* — a maximally visible reading room is a failed library, not a successful one. Quality of mutual visibility for civil inattention purposes (this is a candidate L42b for Sprint 2 — currently not represented).
- Surveillance visibility from *outside* the social setting (security cameras, observation rooms, one-way mirrors). The construct names *mutual* visibility among co-present occupants; one-way visibility is a different ontological object.
- Auditory co-awareness (overhearing, acoustic transparency). These belong to a separable acoustic-visibility construct not represented in L41–L58.
- Pure outdoor sightline length (a panoramic mountain view) that does not connect co-present social actors. Long views without people to be seen are scenically valuable but do not score on L42.
- Visibility-affording lighting conditions (a dim room may have full geometric sightlines but low effective visibility); the construct here is geometric, with lighting handled by a separate luminance-level upstream observable.

### factor_associations
- V2.6 `Latent_Indicators` factor *Visual Co-Presence* (rating items: "I can see most other people here from where I am sitting"; "Most other people here can see me"; "There are few visual barriers between me and others"). Behavioural proxies (head-orientation distribution, glance-rate) belong in extractor docstrings, not the registry.

### extraction_notes_2d
The panel-mandated operationalisation is isovist computation (Hillier & Hanson, 1984), not pixel-level openness. The image alone does not determine an isovist; the registry consumer must augment with a floor-plan (`extractability.requires_floor_plan: true`) from which a 2D polygon of the setting region can be derived. Upstream tags expected to be consumed: `affordance.isovist_area` (the canonical configurational substrate, vantage-sampled and aggregated), `geometry.partition_height_field` (heights of all opaque partitions in the region, used to subtract from the isovist polygon at eye-level), `geometry.glazing_transparency_mask` (boolean field marking glazed surfaces as visually transparent for isovist purposes), and `affordance.sightline_length_distribution` (distribution of free-sightline lengths from a sampled set of seated and standing vantages). The Likert summary is computed by binning the vantage-area-weighted mean isovist area against a corpus-derived distribution. The `region_support: true` flag indicates the extractor should also emit a per-region heatmap when called in region mode. Note: pixel-level openness (a count of "open" pixels in the segmentation) is *not* the right substrate — it conflates open floor with open sightline and over-credits high-ceilinged but partitioned spaces.

### extraction_notes_3d
In the 3D / VR substrate the isovist extends naturally to the 3D isovist or visibility polyhedron. Upstream tags expected: `geometry.mesh_occlusion_field` (the volumetric occluder set), `geometry.eye_height_band` (the slab between approximately 1.1 m and 1.7 m, sampled for seated and standing vantages respectively), and `affordance.isovist_volume_3d` if the extractor produces volumetric rather than slab-cut isovists. The 3D path improves on the 2D-plus-floor-plan path in two respects: it captures partial-height obstruction faithfully (a 1.5 m partition occludes seated but not standing vantages, which the 2D path can only approximate), and it captures transparent surfaces with their actual reflectance/transparency rather than an inferred boolean. Vantage sampling should follow the same convention as the 2D path (area-weighted over the walkable polygon at the chosen eye-height band) so that the resulting Likert summaries are comparable across substrates. Aggregation to the Likert: bin against the same corpus-derived distribution as the 2D path, with a substrate-specific calibration constant logged in the extractor recipe.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
*"Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?"*

I deliberately do not read my Pass 1 definition. Reading only `examples_positive` and `examples_negative`:

The positive examples share three features: (a) co-present persons, (b) physical configurations with low or transparent partitions, long sightlines, or balcony overlooks, and (c) bidirectionality — viewer and viewed are in symmetric positions (each can see the other). The negative examples share an opposing pattern: physical configurations that *foreclose* mutual sightlines (high partitions, high-back booths, alcove walls), even when the floor is fully occupied.

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: A setting-level property describing the geometric opportunity for co-present persons to be visible to and to see one another, varying with partition height, transparency, and sightline depth. Bidirectionality is essential; one-way visibility (negative example 4 implicitly, by contrast with the rejection of outdoor surveillance in scope_excludes) is excluded.

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: Converged. The inferred construct names the same configurational opportunity-structure for mutual perception that the Pass 1 definition_long names, and the bidirectionality requirement is recovered. The civil-inattention/exposure distinction is not recovered from the examples alone, but it is preserved as `scope_excludes` rather than as a definitional commitment, which is the correct location given the panel decision (Sprint-1 compromise: keep L42 as a single tag, document the distinction in `scope_excludes`, defer to L42b in Sprint 2). The blind-read does not recover that distinction because the distinction is, by panel decision, not part of the construct's definition in this sprint.

**Action taken**: None. Examples and definition agree on what the construct measures; the deferred sub-construct is recorded where the panel directed it to be recorded.
