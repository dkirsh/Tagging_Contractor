## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Support for multiple distinct interaction-type micro-zones coexisting within one setting.

### definition_long
Interaction Diversity Potential names the degree to which a single observable setting affords *several distinct kinds* of social interaction at once — for example, a plaza that simultaneously supports solo perching, pair conversation, small-group lingering, and ad-hoc presentation. The construct is a property of the setting's compositional richness rather than of any one affordance: it measures whether a space contains a *variety* of micro-zones with differentiated furniture, postures, distances, and orientations, such that different interaction types can take place in parallel without interfering with one another. Whyte's plaza studies (Whyte, 1980) demonstrated that successful public spaces are ones in which sittable edges, movable seating, and varied micro-edges produce a portfolio of co-existing uses; Oldenburg (1989) argued that "third places" earn their generative role precisely through their accommodation of mixed regulars, drop-ins, and watchers; and Gehl (2010) showed that activity diversity is itself a measurable quality of public life. L58 thus encodes the *coexistence* of affordances, not their sum.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A plaza with movable cafe chairs at the centre, fixed bench-edges along the rim, a low wall used as perch seating, and a planter ledge at table height — all visible in one frame, supporting solo, pair, small-group, and standing-cluster interactions simultaneously.
2. A library reading room that offers high-back individual carrels, a long shared table, two small clustered armchair pods, and a windowed window-seat — four distinct posture/distance regimes coexisting in one room.
3. A hotel lobby containing a check-in counter (focused service), a lounge cluster of armchairs (small-group conversation), a long communal table (mixed laptop and pair work), and a window banquette (perching and watching).
4. A café whose layout combines a counter bar (solo perch + transactional talk), two-tops along the wall (pair conversation), a back banquette suitable for groups of four to six, and a window counter for solo work.
5. A university quad where benches face inward in clusters, low retaining walls double as informal seating, a fountain edge supports perching, and an open lawn affords lying or sprawling — multiple interaction modalities in one observable setting.
6. A community-centre multipurpose room set up with a small ring of chairs in one corner, a long workshop table in another, easels and standing room near a wall, and movable cushions stacked nearby — affording several simultaneous group configurations.
7. A train-station concourse with fixed bench rows (waiting solo or pair), a coffee kiosk with stools (transactional and brief talk), an open standing area used for meeting and parting, and an elevated mezzanine with rail-leaning room.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A long, uniform row of identical airport gate seats — visually rich-looking but offering only one interaction type (solo or pair waiting in a single fixed posture/distance regime).
2. A formal dining room with a single long table and matched chairs — high quality, possibly large capacity, but only one interaction modality is afforded.
3. A movie theatre with rows of identical fixed seats — densely populated but monomodal: forward-focused, low-talk, fixed distance.
4. A classroom with desks in straight rows facing a board — looks "full of seating" but supports a single interaction regime (one-to-many didactic).
5. A large empty hall with no furniture — absence of interaction-type micro-zones, regardless of footprint.
6. A waiting-room corridor with bench seating along both walls — bilateral but monomodal.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Settings where multiple interaction regimes are visibly afforded *in parallel*, even if not all are currently occupied.
- Settings whose diversity comes from *micro-zone variation* (different furniture types, ledges, edges, levels) rather than from sheer footprint.
- Settings where movable furniture is present in a way that signals reconfigurability across interaction types.
- Outdoor public spaces (plazas, parks, courtyards) where edges, ledges, lawns, and movable elements multiply affordance types.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- L58 is not a sum of other latents; it specifically measures *coexistence* of multiple interaction-type affordances within one observable setting. A high score on L44 (sociopetal) plus a high score on L51 (group-assembly) does not automatically yield a high L58 — they may describe the same micro-zone.
- Sheer capacity or seat count: a 200-seat auditorium with one configuration is monomodal and scores low.
- Visual or material variety that does not translate to *interaction-type* variety: a colourful but uniform-seating café scores low.
- Sequential reconfigurability over time (e.g., a hall convertible between layouts) — L58 is about *simultaneous* coexistence, not temporal flexibility.
- Spaces too small to sustain more than one interaction zone at once, regardless of furniture quality.

### factor_associations
- V2.6 Latent_Indicators sheet: composite indicator built from (a) count of distinct seating types visible, (b) count of distinct micro-zones inferred, (c) variation in postures/distances afforded, (d) presence of differentiated edges, ledges, and levels.
- This is a meta-construct over the L41–L57 affordance family; it is operationally a *count + variety* index rather than a single-affordance Likert.

## Engineer pass — extractability, method, BN

### value_type assignment
- `value_type`: ordinal
- `value_range`: `[0, 4]`
- `unit`: `likert_0_4`
- `secondary_value_type`: (omitted — primary is ordinal)

The ordinal Likert is appropriate here despite the panel's general scepticism of the Likert default, because L58 is genuinely a quantity of *richness* and admits a monotonic interpretation (more diverse = higher), unlike the F-formation latents (L44, L48, L49) for which categorical encoding was mandated. The 0–4 scale corresponds roughly to: 0 = monomodal; 1 = one dominant mode plus a weak second; 2 = two clear modes; 3 = three clear modes; 4 = four or more clear coexisting modes.

### extraction_notes_2d
2D extraction proceeds compositely. From upstream observables: (a) `furniture.class_inventory` (a multiset of detected furniture classes — chairs, benches, ledges, tables, stools, perches), (b) `affordance.micro_zone_segmentation` (region-level clustering of the scene into furniture-coherent sub-regions), (c) `posture.afforded_set` (the set of postures inferred to be supported across micro-zones — sitting upright, perching, lounging, standing, leaning), (d) `affordance.distance_regime_set` (the set of inferred interpersonal-distance regimes the layout supports, drawing on Hall's zones), and (e) `geometry.level_variation` (presence of steps, ledges, mezzanines, sunken zones). The L58 extractor counts the cardinality of *distinct interaction-type clusters* in the cross-product of these — a chair-plus-table cluster at conversational distance is one type; a perch-ledge at watching-distance is another. The 2D `partial` extractability reflects that one frame typically misses the back half of the space; the floor-plan augmentation is what enables a confident enumeration. Cultural-calibration warning: the *expected* baseline of diversity in successful public spaces is heavily Western (Whyte's New York plazas, Gehl's Copenhagen squares); spaces designed under different traditions (Japanese tea-house compounds, Middle-Eastern majlis, Latin American sobremesa-oriented patios) may exhibit diversity through different signals (level changes, textile/cushion variation, tatami zoning) that the upstream furniture-class detectors may not register.

### extraction_notes_3d
3D/VR extraction is markedly stronger because the full spatial envelope is observable. From upstream observables: (a) `geometry.room_segmentation_3d` partitions the volume into furniture-coherent sub-regions; (b) `geometry.isovist_field` measures connectivity between micro-zones and confirms that they are simultaneously usable rather than mutually obstructive; (c) `furniture.class_inventory_3d` enumerates seating, perching, and surface affordances with full visibility (no occluded back rows); (d) `affordance.posture_set_3d` reads furniture height, depth, and back support to infer the posture each zone affords; (e) `geometry.connectivity_graph` (the configurational substrate flagged by `configurational_measure: connectivity`) confirms that the micro-zones are co-accessible from a common circulation path. The 3D extractor can compute a true cardinality of distinct interaction-type micro-zones rather than a partial-view estimate, which is why `from_3d_vr: yes`. Floor-plan augmentation is still useful for very large spaces that exceed the VR session's capture radius, but not strictly required.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
*"Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?"*

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: Reading only the seven positives and six negatives, the construct that emerges is "settings that simultaneously support multiple distinct kinds of social use, distinguished by furniture type, posture regime, and group size, within one observable space." The negatives (uniform airport seating, single-table dining room, fixed-row theatre, straight-row classroom, empty hall, mirrored corridor) all share the property of being monomodal despite varying capacity and material quality — which sharpens the inference: the construct is about *kind variety*, not seat count, not material richness, not footprint. The positives all show parallel coexistence (plaza with rim+centre+perch+ledge; library with carrels+long-table+pods+window-seat; hotel with counter+lounge+communal-table+banquette; café with bar+two-tops+banquette+window-counter; quad with benches+walls+fountain+lawn; community room with chair-ring+workshop-table+easel-area+cushion-pile; concourse with bench-rows+kiosk-stools+open-area+mezzanine-rail). The pattern is unambiguous.

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: Converged. The inferred construct ("simultaneously support multiple distinct kinds of social use") matches the Pass 1 `definition_short` ("Support for multiple distinct interaction-type micro-zones coexisting within one setting") almost word-for-word. The `definition_long`'s emphasis on *coexistence rather than sum*, on *micro-zones*, and on *parallel non-interfering use* are all recoverable from the examples without inspection of the definition.

**Action taken**: None. Examples and scope language are sufficient to recover the construct.
