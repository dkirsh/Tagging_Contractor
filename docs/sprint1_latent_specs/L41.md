## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Likelihood the spatial layout produces unplanned co-presence among inhabitants.

### definition_long
Chance-Encounter Potential indexes the rate at which a spatial layout brings inhabitants into unplanned co-presence as a by-product of routine movement. It is a property of the *whole* configuration — the system of paths, intersections, and shared circulation — rather than of any single room or vista. Following Hillier and Hanson's natural-movement theorem (Hillier, 1996, ch. 4; Hillier & Hanson, 1984), the construct predicts encounter rates from the integration value of a space within the global axial map: highly integrated spaces draw through-movement, through-movement multiplies the chance that any two inhabitants will share the space at the same time, and shared presence is the precondition for unplanned interaction (Whyte, 1980, observed the same effect at the urban-plaza scale). Because integration is a global graph-theoretic measure of the configuration, chance-encounter potential is *predicted from* configurational measures of the whole spatial system and is not, in principle, recoverable from a single still image regardless of richness — this is the core reason the panel demoted L41 from V2.6 Tier 2 to Tier 3.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A central atrium in an academic building where corridors from three wings converge at a coffee bar; people from different departments routinely cross paths without planning to.
2. A library reading room positioned at the most integrated node of the building's axial map, so that anyone moving between the entrance, stacks, and study carrels passes through it.
3. A village square that sits at the convergence of the four most-travelled streets, where the same residents bump into each other in the course of unrelated errands.
4. An open-plan office whose central spine corridor is the shortest path between the kitchen, the printer, and the meeting rooms, generating frequent incidental contact between teams that do not formally collaborate.
5. A university quad whose diagonal paths force pedestrians from the dining hall, library, and dormitories to traverse a shared central lawn, producing daily incidental encounters across cohorts.
6. A hospital ward designed with a central nurses' station at the intersection of two patient-room wings and the staff lounge, so that physicians, nurses, and family members repeatedly cross paths in the course of routine movement.
7. A neighbourhood whose street grid produces a single highly integrated commercial street through which most residents pass on the way between home and transit, generating the kind of weak-tie recognition Whyte (1980) documented on Manhattan plazas.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A cul-de-sac residential street: visually open with sightlines and shared sidewalks, but topologically segregated — residents who live there encounter only each other, never through-movement strangers.
2. A long single-loaded corridor with offices only on one side and no destinations along its length: visible paths exist but no convergence, so encounters are rare and dyadic rather than chance.
3. A spacious lobby with a single revolving door and a single elevator bank directly opposite: ample shared circulation but no branching, so the same handful of people are funneled along the same line with no real chance of *unplanned* encounter beyond the elevator wait.
4. A bustling cafeteria at lunch hour: lots of co-presence and visible intersections, but the co-presence is *planned* (everyone is there to eat), not the by-product of unrelated routine movement that the construct names.
5. A wide outdoor plaza visible in a single photograph showing many crossing paths: looks integrative, but if the plaza is at the topological dead-end of a peninsula in the surrounding street network, integration is low and chance-encounter potential is correspondingly low — the cue language of "multiple paths crossing" is misleading without the configurational measure.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Settings whose chance-encounter potential is partly accidental — a building designed for one purpose whose corridor topology happens to generate high integration along a non-designed route.
- Outdoor as well as indoor configurations: streets, plazas, courtyards, and quads are within scope provided a configurational measure can be computed.
- Cases where the *opportunity* for encounter exists even when no inhabitants are visible in the image — the construct is an affordance of the layout, not a count of people present.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- Planned co-presence (a meeting room during a meeting, a classroom during a lecture, a queue at a service counter) — these are focused-encounter settings governed by L46/L47/L52, not by L41.
- Conversation-quality or interaction-richness judgments — L41 measures the *opportunity for unplanned co-presence*, not whether the resulting encounters are warm, prolonged, or substantive.
- Single-image judgments untethered from any configurational measure — if no floor plan or topological graph is available, L41 cannot be assigned a defensible value (this is the Tier 3 commitment).

### extraction_notes_2d
A 2D image alone cannot ground L41: the construct depends on global integration in the axial map of the surrounding configuration, and that map is not visible in any single photograph (this is the panel's substantive claim, citing Hillier, 1996, ch. 4). If only an image is available, the extractor MUST return `null` with confidence 0 and a flag indicating `requires_floor_plan: true`. When a floor plan is available alongside the image, extraction proceeds in two steps: (i) use upstream tags `affordance.path_count_visible` and `affordance.intersection_density` to localise the depicted region within the floor plan; (ii) read the precomputed `configuration.axial_integration_global` value for that region from the floor-plan-derived axial map and bin it into the 0–4 Likert. The image-derived cues from V2.6 — "multiple paths crossing", "central nodes", "visible intersections", "shared circulation" — are *suggestive proxies only*, not extraction substrates; the extractor MUST NOT return a non-null value on the basis of those proxies alone. Cross-cultural variance is `medium`: the configurational measure itself is culturally neutral, but encounter *thresholds* (what counts as a "high" rate of unplanned co-presence) vary by setting type and culture.

### extraction_notes_3d
A 3D/VR scan provides richer geometry than a 2D image but does not by itself supply the configurational measure either: a single-room scan still lacks the global axial map. Extraction from 3D therefore mirrors the 2D protocol with one upgrade: the scan can be used to *derive* the relevant slice of the axial map locally, via upstream tags `geometry.room_polygon_3d`, `geometry.threshold_segments_3d`, and `affordance.sightline_extent_3d`. If the scan covers the entire building (rare in V2.6-source corpora but possible in BIM-augmented settings), the extractor can compute `configuration.axial_integration_global` directly and bin it as in the 2D-plus-floor-plan path. If the scan covers only part of the configuration, the extractor MUST mark the result as bounded by the scan extent and downgrade confidence accordingly. Isovist area (upstream tag `affordance.isovist_area`) is *not* a substitute for integration in this latent — it correlates weakly with chance-encounter potential because it indexes visibility from a single point rather than through-movement across the system.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
*Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?*

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: The positives all share a structural feature: a space sits at a topological convergence point in a larger network (atrium of three wings, central reading room, village square at four-street junction, office spine between kitchen-printer-meeting-rooms, university quad on diagonal paths, hospital nurses' station at wing intersection, integrated commercial street). The encounters described are *unplanned* and arise as a *by-product* of unrelated routine movement. The negatives crucially distinguish (a) topological segregation despite visual openness (cul-de-sac), (b) visible paths without convergence (single-loaded corridor), (c) shared circulation without branching (single-elevator lobby), (d) co-presence that is *planned* not chance (cafeteria at lunch), and (e) the failure mode of inferring the construct from image cues alone when global topology disagrees (peninsula plaza). I infer the construct: *the propensity of a spatial configuration to generate unplanned co-presence among unrelated inhabitants by virtue of its topological position in the surrounding network of paths*.

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: Converged. The blind-read recovers the three load-bearing claims — (i) unplanned co-presence, (ii) by-product of routine movement, (iii) configurational rather than local property — and recovers the negative-example logic that distinguishes L41 from focused-encounter settings (cafeteria), from low-integration settings that *look* integrative (peninsula plaza), and from co-presence that is not unplanned (queue/meeting). The Tier 3 commitment is also implicit in the negatives: examples 4 and 5 specifically punish image-only inference.
