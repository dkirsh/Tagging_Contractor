## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Affordance for adjacent-but-not-incorporated presence: watching, listening, lingering on edges.

### definition_long
The degree to which a setting affords *peripheral participation* — the bodily posture of being adjacent to a focused activity or social flow without joining it. Operationally, the construct is the spatial-physical provision of perches, edges, benches facing flow, café-window stools, balcony rails, and similar fittings that allow a person to remain proximate to action while abstaining from full engagement (Whyte, 1980, ch. 2). The term was introduced by Lave and Wenger (1991) to describe a developmental trajectory in apprenticeship learning; this registry adopts only the *spatial-physical* sense — the affordance for a bystander posture — and treats the developmental sense as out of scope for image-extracted tagging. The construct is closely related to Goffman's (1963) notion of unfocused interaction, in which co-present persons monitor one another at the threshold of involvement without committing to a focused encounter.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A café with a continuous window-counter and bar stools facing the sidewalk, where solo patrons sit watching pedestrian flow without entering it.
2. A library reading-room with a low rail along a mezzanine, where a person leans on the rail looking down at the activity below.
3. A plaza ringed with benches whose backs are to a planter and whose seat-fronts face the central paved area where children play and adults walk.
4. A theatre lobby with a velvet-rope perch along one wall, allowing a guest to stand and watch arrivals without joining any of the conversational clusters.
5. A wide hallway with a recessed bay containing a built-in bench, where a student sits with a laptop while seminars let out into the corridor.
6. A train-station mezzanine with a knee-height rail overlooking the concourse, used by people who have arrived early and watch the boards and the crowd.
7. An open-plan office "touch-down" counter at the edge of the kitchen area, where a person stands with coffee monitoring the kitchen conversation without entering the F-formation.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A row of dining chairs around a single table — these face *each other*, supporting a focused F-formation, not adjacency to a separate flow.
2. A single armchair in the geometric centre of an empty room — there is no flow to be peripheral *to*, and no edge from which to be peripheral.
3. A queue of standing people facing one direction at a service counter — they are *participants* in a service script (L46), not bystanders to it.
4. A circle of benches around a fountain with backs to the surrounding plaza — this affords inward-facing group seating, not edge-perching toward an outer flow.
5. A standing-mingling room with no perches, edges, or sightlines onto a separate flow — this affords mingling (L56) but not peripheral participation, because there is nothing peripheral *to*.
6. A solo lounge chair in a cul-de-sac alcove with no view of any flow — this affords retreat or refuge, not peripheral participation.

### scope_includes
- Edges, perches, rails, and counters whose primary affordance is *facing* a separable flow or activity from the periphery.
- Bench arrangements oriented toward a circulation path or open activity area rather than toward each other.
- Window-seating in cafés, lounges, lobbies that places the seated person at the boundary of the building's activity and the street's activity.
- Mezzanines, balconies, and stair-landings with rails or seating overlooking a lower-level activity.
- Recessed wall-bays, niches, and "touch-down" surfaces along corridors or kitchens that allow lingering at the edge of moving traffic.

### scope_excludes
- Inward-facing seating arrangements that constitute their own focal group (L44, L48, L49).
- Settings that afford only mingling without any perch, edge, or rail to support adjacent-but-not-incorporated lingering (L56).
- Solo retreat or refuge alcoves with no view of any flow (would be a different construct, not in L41–L58).
- The Lave-and-Wenger developmental sense of *legitimate peripheral participation* as a learning trajectory; this registry treats only the spatial-physical affordance.
- Chance-encounter potential as a configurational property of the whole spatial system (L41).

### extraction_notes_2d
Score by composing four upstream observables: (a) `affordance.perch_count` — count of stools, low rails, recessed bench-bays, and window-counter positions detected in the image; (b) `affordance.edge_seating_orientation` — ratio of seated-furniture detections whose front-vector points away from interior focal clusters and toward a circulation path or window; (c) `affordance.sightline_to_flow` — presence of an unobstructed view from each candidate perch to a separable activity flow (street, concourse, kitchen, plaza); (d) `affordance.rail_balcony_presence` — detection of horizontal rails, mezzanine edges, or balcony enclosures at standing-elbow height. The four are combined into a 0–4 ordinal: 0 = no perches and no edge-oriented seating; 1 = one or two incidental perches; 2 = a clear single-perch affordance with sightline; 3 = multiple perches with sightlines distributed along an edge; 4 = a continuous edge-affordance system (e.g., café window-counter the full length of a wall, or mezzanine rail with seating). Western-calibration warning is *not* required for L45 (cross-cultural variance flagged medium), but extractors should note that perch fittings (window-counter culture, balcony-rail culture) vary somewhat by tradition; the affordance itself is broadly recognised across cultures (Whyte's findings replicated in non-Western plazas).

### extraction_notes_3d
In a 3D or VR scene, use furniture-class metadata to identify perch-typed objects (bar stools, window stools, low rails, recessed bench-bays) and use scene-graph geometry to compute, for each perch, the angular sweep of unobstructed sightline to nearby circulation paths or activity zones. The 0–4 ordinal is then computed from (perch count) × (mean sightline-to-flow angular sweep) × (boolean for continuous edge system present). Where the scene contains an explicit annotated `circulation_path` polyline or `activity_zone` polygon, those tags should be used directly; where they are not present, derive a proxy circulation path from the longest straight uninterrupted floor-segment and a proxy activity zone from the largest open floor area not enclosed by an F-formation furniture cluster. Edges where the perch faces *into* an F-formation (L44/L48/L49) rather than *toward* a separable flow should not count.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
*"Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?"*

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: Reading only the seven positive examples and six negative examples without consulting `definition_short` or `definition_long`, the construct that emerges is: *settings that provide a perch, rail, edge, or bench oriented toward a separable flow of activity, allowing a person to remain physically adjacent to the activity while abstaining from joining it*. The negatives clarify the boundaries: face-to-face seating arrangements are excluded (these are F-formations), centred solo seating with no flow is excluded (no flow to be peripheral to), service-counter queues are excluded (these are participants not bystanders), inward-facing benches around a fountain are excluded (these face inward not outward), and mingling rooms without perches are excluded (mingling is not peripheral participation).

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: The blind-read inference converges with Pass 1's `definition_short` ("Affordance for adjacent-but-not-incorporated presence: watching, listening, lingering on edges") and `definition_long` (which emphasises the spatial-physical provision of perches, edges, etc.). The blind-read recovers the *adjacent-but-not-incorporated* core, the *requires-a-flow-to-be-peripheral-to* boundary, and the *not-the-same-as-mingling* boundary. It does NOT recover the explicit reference to the Lave-and-Wenger origin of the term, but that is metadata about provenance, not about the construct itself.

**Action taken**: None. The examples as written successfully encode the construct.

### extraction_notes
No V2.6 Tier override for L45 (V2.6 Tier 1 retained as final Tier 1). The principal disambiguation made in this spec is the choice to scope L45 to its *spatial-physical* sense (perches, edges, rails facing a flow) rather than its *developmental* sense (apprentice trajectory toward full participation). The spatial sense matches the V2.6 `Observable 2D Cues` row ("Perches on edges; benches facing flow; café window seating; balcony rails") unambiguously. The developmental sense — Lave & Wenger's original meaning — is a learning trajectory that cannot be tagged from a single still image regardless of richness, and was almost certainly not intended by the V2.6 author for this row. The decision to cite both Lave & Wenger (for term provenance) and Whyte (for the spatial sense actually adopted) honours the special-notes instruction in the assignment brief.

L45 is explicitly distinguished from three sibling tags in the same neighbourhood: (i) L41 chance-encounter potential is configurational and not image-extractable; L45 is local-affordance and image-extractable. (ii) L56 mingling affordance requires standing-circulating space without a sustained focal flow; L45 requires a perch-or-edge fitting *and* a separable flow. (iii) L44/L48/L49 F-formation latents are inward-facing; L45 perches are outward-facing toward a non-incorporated flow.
