## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Ease of entering or leaving a bounded social region, indexed by convex-map depth.

### definition_long
Boundary Permeability is the ease with which a person can enter or exit a socially bounded region — a room, an alcove, a courtyard, a plaza interior — without paying a high access cost in either configurational depth, perceptual surveillance, or normative threshold control. Following Hillier and Hanson (1984), the construct is operationalised as *depth in the convex map*: the number of convex-space transitions required to traverse from a defined exterior to the interior in question. Shallower regions (depth 1–2) are highly permeable; deeper regions (depth ≥ 4) are impermeable in the configurational sense, regardless of how many doors or wide thresholds the photographed boundary itself displays. The convex-map account explains why a sociologically open plaza (Whyte, 1980) and a sociologically closed inner sanctum can look superficially similar in a single image: the difference lives in the global topology of access, not in the local visual openness of a single threshold. Goffman (1963, pp. 151–164) supplies the second leg of the construct — the ritual permission to cross — which colours but does not determine the configurational depth.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A ground-floor café whose seating area is one convex step from the public sidewalk through a wide retracted door — depth 1, no normative gating, high permeability.
2. A plaza with multiple low-curb entrances on three sides, where any pedestrian on any adjacent street can step into the interior in a single transition.
3. A hotel lobby whose lounge area is reached from the porte-cochère through a single open archway — convex depth 2, no door swing, no reception barrier between exterior and lounge.
4. A library reading room whose entry is a wide arched opening from a flanking corridor that itself opens directly to the foyer — total convex depth 3 with no doors at any transition.
5. A market hall arcade whose stalls are accessible from the surrounding street through any of six unmanned arched openings, with partial knee-wall partitions but no full enclosure.
6. An open-plan museum gallery reachable from the main hall via a single threshold-less floor change — depth 1 with only material articulation marking the boundary.
7. A university quad with four diagonal paths converging on a central lawn, each path entering through a gap in low hedging — multiple shallow entrances, no normative gating.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. *Visually open but configurationally deep*: A glass-walled conference room visible across three intervening rooms from the building entrance — looks open in the photograph, but convex-map depth is 4. Permeability is low despite the visual cue.
2. *Visually closed but configurationally shallow*: A small reading nook entered through a single narrow wood door directly off the main lobby — depth 1, but the visible "closed door" cue would misclassify it as impermeable.
3. *High exterior visibility, normatively gated*: An office reception fronted by floor-to-ceiling glass directly off the street, but the door is keycard-locked and a turnstile sits behind it. The convex transition exists but is normatively impermeable.
4. *Permeable threshold, surveilled interior*: A courtyard entered through a wide unlocked gate, but with a security desk visible from the gate that issues badges to all entrants. Permeable in geometry, gated in social practice.
5. *Inverse case for L42 distinction*: A reading room with full sightlines from the corridor (high interactional visibility) but reached only via a stair to a third floor and then through two intervening rooms — the visibility is high, the permeability is low.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Configurational depth on a convex map computed from the floor plan, including outdoor convex spaces (plazas, courtyards) when the plan is drawn at appropriate scale.
- Normative boundary control (security desks, hosts, ticket booths, badge readers) as a qualifier that *reduces* permeability without changing convex depth — encoded as a downward adjustment in the Likert score.
- Multi-entrance topologies where a single region has more than one convex link to the exterior — these *increase* permeability, captured by the convex map's connectivity along with depth.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- *Sightline openness alone* — visual transparency to a region from outside is the province of L42 (Interactional Visibility), not L54. A glass-walled but configurationally deep room is L42-high, L54-low.
- *Density of foot traffic actually observed* in the image — that is downstream evidence about realised use (closer to L41, Chance-Encounter Potential), not about the boundary's permeability as a structural property.
- *Door-swing direction or hardware* per se — a heavy door is no less a single convex transition than a light one; the construct lives at the convex-map level, not the affordance-detail level.
- *Exit affordance under emergency conditions* — life-safety egress is governed by code requirements that are largely orthogonal to the social-encounter construct of permeability.

### extraction_notes_2d
A single image is insufficient to score L54 reliably; the panel-mandated `requires_floor_plan: true` flag governs. Where only an image is available, the extractor must combine local cues with floor-plan augmentation. Local upstream observables expected: `affordance.threshold_count` (number of distinct apertures visible at the boundary of the region), `affordance.threshold_aperture_width` (clear-width estimate per aperture), `affordance.door_state` (open / partial / closed / absent), `affordance.barrier_height` (knee-wall vs. waist-wall vs. full partition), and `affordance.normative_control_present` (security desk, host stand, ticket booth, turnstile, locked-hardware indicator). These cues are *visual surrogates* for the underlying convex-map depth and will systematically misclassify the two failure modes documented in `examples_negative` 1 and 2: visually open but configurationally deep, and visually closed but configurationally shallow. The extractor must therefore lower its confidence sharply when the floor plan is absent and the local cues are ambiguous. Critical Spohn-rule note: L54's `bn.parent_tags` deliberately exclude pure sightline observables (e.g. `affordance.isovist_area`, `affordance.transparent_surface_fraction`), which belong to L42 — overlap of more than 50% with L42 will trigger the `audit-extraction-plan` validator.

### extraction_notes_3d
With a 3D model or VR walkthrough, the convex partition can be computed directly: segment the navigable floor surface into maximal convex regions, build the adjacency graph, and compute geodesic depth from each exterior-flagged region to each interior region of interest. The depth value, mapped through a monotonic compression (e.g. depth 1 → 4, depth 2 → 3, depth 3 → 2, depth 4 → 1, depth ≥ 5 → 0), yields the geometric component of the Likert score. Connectivity (number of distinct shortest paths of equal depth) provides the multi-entrance bonus. Normative-control affordances detected during the walkthrough (turnstiles, manned reception, badge readers) are applied as a multiplicative discount before final ordinal binning. When only monocular-3D approximation is available, depth estimation degrades to "partial" because depth-from-defocus and monocular-depth nets recover *visible* depth but cannot recover the topology of unseen rooms beyond the photographed boundary; this is why `monocular_3d_approx` is set to `partial` and not `yes` despite the underlying construct being natively geometric.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
*"Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?"*

Reading examples_positive 1–7 and examples_negative 1–5 only, with definition_short and definition_long set aside:

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: The examples cluster around two co-varying dimensions — (a) how many spatial transitions separate an interior region from the exterior public realm, and (b) whether each transition is gated, either by physical hardware or by normative control (a host, a desk, a guard). High scores correspond to *few transitions, no gating*; low scores correspond to *many transitions OR strong gating*. The negative examples specifically isolate two distractors: visual openness without configurational shallowness, and visual closure with configurational shallowness — which means the construct is NOT visibility per se. The fifth negative example explicitly contrasts with L42 (interactional visibility), confirming that the construct is permeability of access, not openness of sight.

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: Converged. The blind-read recovers (i) configurational depth as the primary dimension, (ii) normative control as a qualifying dimension, (iii) the explicit disjointness from sightline-based L42, and (iv) the multi-entrance bonus implied by examples_positive 2 and 7. The only mild gap is that the blind-reader did not spontaneously name "convex map" as the technical substrate — but the examples consistently use language about "transitions" and "convex steps" implicitly, and the count-of-transitions dimension is recovered.

**Action taken**: None required. Examples sufficiently specify the construct; the technical name "convex map" is a vocabulary choice that the definition supplies and the examples need not.
