## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short

Setting affordance for task-focused coordination around a shared artifact or work surface.

### definition_long

Collaborative Work Interaction indexes the degree to which a setting's furniture, surfaces, and tools jointly afford *task-focused coordination* — two or more co-present persons oriented not toward each other, but toward a shared task object that mediates their joint activity (Hutchins, 1995, ch. 3 on the "cockpit as cognitive system"). The construct is grounded in the distributed-cognition tradition's claim that coordination is *constituted* by the configuration of artifacts in the local material field: a writable surface, a co-viewable display, a tool laid between two workers, all enable joint attention and propagate representational state across participants in a way that mere co-presence cannot (Suchman, 1987, ch. 5; Kirsh, 1995, on the intelligent use of space). Geometrically, the canonical realisation is Kendon's (1990, pp. 209ff) *L-arrangement F-formation*: two or more participants positioned at right or oblique angles such that the o-space contains a horizontal task surface, with bodies oriented toward that surface rather than toward each other in vis-à-vis. The construct is therefore *not* "small-group conversation" (L49) — it requires *task artifacts* and a shared horizontal or co-viewable surface, not merely clusterable furniture; the discriminating cue is the presence of a shared work surface oriented for co-viewing.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)

1. Two designers seated at a 90° corner of a large drafting table with a single sheet of paper spread between them, each oriented toward the paper rather than toward the other; pencils, rulers, and reference photographs lie on the shared surface within arm's reach of both.
2. A library group-study room with a rectangular table, four chairs on two long sides, a wall-mounted whiteboard at one end, and a shared laptop in the centre — bodies face the table or the whiteboard, not each other.
3. A clinical examination bay where a physician and a resident stand on the same side of a wheeled cart, both looking down at an open patient chart and a tablet displaying imaging; the cart is the shared task surface and the L-arrangement places them shoulder-to-shoulder around it.
4. An open-plan engineering bench with a long workbench, two stools side-by-side facing the bench, an oscilloscope screen placed between the two stools at eye level, and a circuit board wired between them.
5. A kitchen counter prep area where two cooks stand side-by-side along the counter facing the same cutting boards and ingredient bowls, with shared knives and a recipe card propped against the backsplash; their work surface is continuous and co-viewable.
6. A classroom maker-station with a large central tabletop, four high stools positioned around it at L and side-by-side angles, scattered components and tools on the surface, and a wall-mounted shared display visible from every stool.
7. A control-room console where two operators sit side-by-side at a long desk with multiple monitors arranged in a coordinated viewing arc, shared keyboard-and-mouse zones, and reference manuals on a shelf above — Hutchins' canonical distributed-cognition setting.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)

1. A circle of café chairs around a small round table with only coffee cups on the table — multi-seat, sociopetal, but the table holds *consumption objects*, not task artifacts; this is L44 (sociopetal seating) and L49 (small-group conversation), not L50.
2. A pair of armchairs angled vis-à-vis across a low coffee table in a hotel lobby — face-to-face conversational geometry with no shared work surface; the table is at coffee-cup height and not oriented for joint co-viewing of task content; L44/L48, not L50.
3. A row of individual workstations in an open-plan office, each with its own monitor, keyboard, and locked-down personal task surface — workers are co-present but no surface is *shared*; this is parallel solo work, not collaborative work.
4. A long boardroom table with twelve chairs, all facing inward, and a single document at the centre that no participant can actually reach or co-view at working distance — looks like a shared-task setting but the geometry forecloses joint co-viewing; reads as L52 (presentation) when a chair-of-meeting is implied.
5. A study carrel with a desk, a single chair, and a laptop — even if a second person could pull a chair alongside, the carrel is dimensioned for solo work and offers no L-arrangement seating slot.
6. A theatre lobby with high cocktail tables and stools — the tables are shared surfaces but they hold drinks, not task artifacts; without writable surface, tool availability, or co-viewable display, the setting affords mingling (L56), not collaborative work.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)

- Settings with a shared horizontal work surface (table, bench, counter, console) of sufficient depth to be co-viewed and co-manipulated by two or more participants whose chair or stool positions place them at L, side-by-side, or semicircular angles around the surface.
- Settings where the shared task object is a wall-mounted or large-format display (whiteboard, monitor, plotter output) co-viewable from multiple seating or standing positions, even when no horizontal surface is present.
- Settings where tool availability — pens, markers, instruments, components, reference materials — is distributed across the shared surface in a way that signals expected joint manipulation, per Kirsh (1995) on the intelligent use of space.
- Hybrid horizontal-plus-vertical arrangements (a table with an attached whiteboard) where the geometry supports both surface-level co-manipulation and display-level co-viewing.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)

- Conversational seating clusters whose surface holds only consumption objects (coffee, drinks, snacks); these are L44/L48/L49 even when the chairs are clustered. The discriminating cue is the *content* of the shared surface, not the chair geometry.
- Parallel-solo workstations where each person has a private surface and no shared horizontal or vertical artifact mediates joint activity; even tightly packed open-plan layouts fail L50 if the surfaces are individually owned.
- One-to-many presentation arrangements (lecture hall, classroom rows) where a single shared display exists but the audience seats face it in parallel ranks rather than around it; this is L52 (presentation), not L50.
- Standing-only mingling settings where shared tables exist but hold drinks and snacks rather than task artifacts; L56, not L50.
- Single-occupant workspaces (carrels, individual desks, solo studios) regardless of how many tools are present — L50 is constitutively a *multi-participant* construct.
- Vis-à-vis face-to-face seating across a table where the table is empty or holds only conversation props — this is L44/L48 (the bodies orient toward each other through the table), not L50 (where bodies orient toward shared content on the table).

### factor_associations

- V2.6 Latent_Indicators sheet, factor cluster F-COLLAB-1 (Collaborative Affordance): rating items "the room supports working together on a shared task," "there are surfaces multiple people can write on or view together," "the tools needed for joint work are at hand," "the seating allows people to gather around a shared object"; behavioral proxy "if two people came in to work on a project together, this room would support them."
- Loads on the V2.6 *task-coordination* superfactor alongside L52 (One-to-Many Presentation) and L53 (Hosting/Service); L50 is distinguished by its *symmetric* multi-participant orientation around a shared horizontal artifact, where L52 is asymmetric (one source, many receivers) and L53 is role-bound (host serves guest).

- `social.sociopetal_seating` (L44) — shared geometric substrate (chair clustering and o-space); L44 is the seating-topology construct, L50 is the work-surface-and-tool affordance that overlays it.
- `social.one_to_many_presentation` (L52) — sibling task-coordination construct; L52 is asymmetric one-source-many-receivers, L50 is symmetric multi-participant around a shared artifact.

The ordinal Likert encodes affordance strength along the dimension *how strongly does this setting support task-focused coordination around a shared artifact*. A score of 0 indicates parallel-solo or consumption-only configurations; 1 indicates a shared surface with no tools or display; 2 indicates a shared surface with tools but ambiguous seating geometry; 3 indicates an L-arrangement or side-by-side seating around a tool-bearing surface; 4 indicates the canonical Hutchins-style configuration with shared surface, distributed tools, co-viewable display, and L-arrangement seating that geometrically guarantees joint orientation toward the task object.

### extraction_notes_2d

The L50 extractor is a composite over four upstream observable families that together discriminate L50 from its sibling F-formation latents (L44, L48, L49). (i) Shared-surface inventory: upstream tags `furniture.table_instances`, `furniture.bench_instances`, `furniture.counter_instances`, plus the per-surface attribute `surface.area_m2` and `surface.height_class` (coffee-cup-height vs. work-height vs. standing-height) — work-height horizontal surfaces of ≥1 m² are the dominant L50 substrate. (ii) Writable-surface and shared-display inventory: upstream tags `affordance.whiteboard_present`, `affordance.large_display_present`, `affordance.writable_surface_count`, plus `affordance.display_co_viewability` (a derived measure of whether the display normal admits acute viewing from ≥2 seat positions). (iii) Tool and task-artifact density: upstream tags `affordance.tool_instance_count_on_surface`, `affordance.task_artifact_present` (papers, components, instruments — anything that signals expected joint manipulation, contrasted with consumption objects like cups, plates, glasses), and `affordance.consumption_object_ratio` (the ratio of consumption to task objects on the surface — a high ratio downgrades L50 toward L44/L49). (iv) Seating-around-surface geometry: upstream tags `furniture.chair_instances` and `furniture.chair_orientation`, combined as `geometry.chair_l_arrangement_count` (the count of chair pairs at 60–120° front-normal angle around a common surface — Kendon's L-arrangement F-formation, Kendon, 1990, pp. 209ff). The discriminating computation against L49 (small-group conversation) is exactly this: L49 fires on chair clustering with vis-à-vis or circular front-normal convergence around an empty or consumption-only o-space; L50 requires that the o-space contain a *task surface* with non-trivial tool/artifact density. The Hutchins (1995) cockpit example is the prototype: the artifact configuration — speed cards, attitude indicators, throttle quadrant — *constitutes* the coordination, and the seating is L-arrangement around it. The V2.6 cue list ("large shared tables; writable surfaces; shared displays; tool availability") corresponds directly to families (i)–(iii); family (iv) (the L-arrangement geometric primitive) is added per the L50-special-note Kendon mandate. Cross-cultural variance is `medium` rather than `high` because collaborative-work conventions are more standardised across cultures than proxemic conversational arrangements; nevertheless downstream consumers should note that the canonical seated-at-table workspace assumed here is itself a Western-industrial form, and floor-seated tatami-style or standing-only collaborative configurations may underscore on the chair-arrangement signal even when the artifact-and-surface signals are strong.

### extraction_notes_3d

In a 3D / VR substrate the four upstream families become metrically clean. (i) Shared-surface inventory: each table/bench/counter is segmented as a 3D solid, surface area is the top-face area in m², and height class is determined directly from the floor-plane offset of the top face — work-height horizontal surfaces of ≥1 m² at 70–110 cm are the L50-canonical substrate. (ii) Writable and display surfaces: whiteboards and large displays are detected as planar near-vertical surfaces with characteristic aspect ratios and (for displays) self-luminance or texture features; the `display_co_viewability` measure becomes a true geometric cone test — does the display normal admit a viewing cone of <60° from at least two chair positions? (iii) Tool and task-artifact density: 3D object detection on the surface allows direct counting and classification of items as task vs. consumption; the consumption-object-ratio measure becomes robust against camera-angle bias. (iv) L-arrangement seating geometry: chair fronts projected onto the floor plane around the shared surface yield exact pairwise angles and exact ray-intersection points; the L50 extractor should emit, alongside its primary ordinal score, the F-formation-geometry sub-field `(o_space_centroid_xy, o_space_radius_m, predominant_arrangement)` per the cross-construct extractor-contract mandate (Kendon, 1990, pp. 209–214; panel disposition §3 ix), with `predominant_arrangement` constrained to take the value `l_arrangement` or `side_by_side` for high-scoring L50 settings (vis-à-vis dominant geometries should downgrade because they fail the around-the-surface-not-across-it constraint). In VR walk-throughs an additional behavioural proxy is available: avatars seated at the L-arrangement positions and prompted to manipulate a task artifact will exhibit measurable joint-attention dwell on the shared surface, gating the geometric score. The `requires_floor_plan: false` setting holds: L50 is a local seat-and-surface property, not a configurational property of the room.

## Notes

### interaction_mode

`focused`. Collaborative work interaction is constitutively a *focused encounter* in Goffman's (1963) sense — a sustained mutual involvement around a shared concern, where the shared concern is the task artifact mediating joint activity. The construct does not apply to unfocused-encounter spaces (waiting areas, transit halls, lobbies) regardless of how many surfaces or chairs are present.

### cross_cultural_variance

`medium`. Collaborative-work conventions are more standardised across cultures than proxemic conversational arrangements (the L43/L44/L48/L49 cluster), reflecting the global convergence of industrial-organisational and educational workspace design. Nevertheless the canonical seated-at-work-height-table configuration assumed here is itself a Western-industrial form; floor-seated tatami-style collaborative configurations and standing-only craft-workshop arrangements may underscore on the chair-arrangement signal even when the artifact-and-surface signals are strong. Downstream model trainers should treat very low scores in non-Western residential or craft settings with appropriate scepticism. The flag is `medium` rather than `high` because the panel's `high` allocation (L43, L44, L48, L49, L55) was reserved for proxemic and territorial constructs with the strongest cross-cultural-variance evidence base.

### extraction_notes

No V2.6 Tier override applied — L50 remains Tier 1 as in V2.6. Two extractor-contract requirements deserve special note for the Phase E stub author: (a) the F-formation-geometry sub-field `(o_space_centroid_xy, o_space_radius_px, predominant_arrangement)` is mandatory output (Kendon panel mandate, panel disposition §3 ix), with the additional L50-specific constraint that `predominant_arrangement` should take the value `l_arrangement` or `side_by_side` for high-scoring L50 settings (vis-à-vis-dominant geometries should downgrade because they fail the around-the-surface-not-across-it constraint that distinguishes L50 from L44/L48); (b) the Hall distance-zone estimate per Hall (1966) is mandatory output (Hall panel mandate, panel disposition §3 viii), with the L50-specific note that distances within the shared-surface o-space are typically intimate-to-personal-near (the bodies are shoulder-to-shoulder around the artifact), not personal-far or social, and a personal-far-or-greater estimate at high L50 score should trigger an extractor warning. Both requirements are runtime extractor-output specifications, not registry-level fields, and so are recorded here rather than in `extractability`. The Hutchins/Kirsh emphasis — that L50 measures the *coordination affordance* of the artifact configuration, not the mere presence of furniture — is operationalised in the parent-set weighting (artifact-and-tool observables dominate chair-geometry observables) and should be preserved in the Phase E extractor design.
