## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short

Two-person seating geometry whose distance and partial enclosure afford intimate dyadic talk.

### definition_long

The spatial-organisational property of a two-seat (or two-slot) arrangement whose seat separation, mutual orientation, and surrounding partial enclosure jointly afford a sustained dyadic encounter at Hall's *personal* or *intimate* distance, supporting low-volume confidential talk between exactly two co-present persons (Hall, 1966, ch. 10; Kendon, 1990, ch. 7). The construct sits at the intersection of two analytic traditions: Hall's proxemic distance zones — which establish that the same two seats afford radically different encounters depending on centre-to-centre separation (intimate 0–18 in / 0–46 cm; personal-close 18–30 in / 46–76 cm; personal-far 30–48 in / 76–122 cm; social-close 48–84 in / 122–213 cm) — and Kendon's F-formation system, which establishes that dyads in `vis_a_vis` and `l_arrangement` configurations enact qualitatively different interactional ecologies than dyads in `side_by_side` arrangements (Kendon, 1990, pp. 209–217; Goffman, 1971, ch. 2). L48 is the *dyad-specific* affordance: it picks out scenes in which the geometry has been shaped to scaffold a two-person encounter — booths, two-seat nooks, paired armchairs in a side alcove, bench-and-armchair corners — rather than the general multi-party seating topology of L44 or the small-group arrangements of L49.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)

1. A high-backed two-person booth in a quiet bistro: bench-front to bench-front across a 24-inch table, the high backs forming partial lateral screens that visually and acoustically separate the dyad from the adjacent tables — a vis-à-vis arrangement at personal-close distance with explicit screening.
2. A pair of armchairs angled at roughly 90° in the corner of a hotel lobby, separated by a small side table at 28-inch centre-to-centre, the corner walls providing two-sided screening and the angle producing an L-arrangement at personal-close distance.
3. A built-in window seat with two facing cushion positions across a low sill-table, the window-bay alcove walls forming a three-sided partial enclosure; vis-à-vis at personal-far distance.
4. A semicircular two-seat banquette tucked into a side alcove of a restaurant, the seats placed at L-arrangement angles around a small round table, with curtain or planter screening on the open side.
5. A pair of stools at the inside corner of an L-shaped bar, knees nearly touching across the corner — vis-à-vis at intimate-to-personal-close distance, with the bar mass and corner geometry providing partial screening from the rest of the bar.
6. Two facing wing-back chairs across a small chess table in a club library, the high wings of each chair forming a partial visual screen against the side aisles; vis-à-vis at personal-close distance.
7. A built-in kitchen breakfast nook with two opposing benches across a narrow table, three sides walled and one side open — vis-à-vis at personal-close distance with three-sided enclosure.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)

1. Two seats among a row of eight on an airport gate-area bench, no intervening table, no partial screening, all seats parallel-facing forward — fails on (a) lack of dyad-specific enclosure, (b) parallel rather than convergent orientation, (c) social-far distance to the next seat enforced by armrests.
2. A pair of armchairs in the centre of a large open hotel lounge facing each other across a coffee table at 60-inch centre-to-centre — the geometry is vis-à-vis but the distance is social-close (Hall, 1966), the surrounding open floor provides no screening, and the configuration reads as a meeting setting rather than an intimate-conversation setting.
3. A four-chair café table with all four chairs occupied by their fronts converging on the table centre — this is L44 sociopetal seating for a four-person F-formation, not a dyad-specific affordance; the construct is L49 (small-group arrangement), not L48.
4. A bench seat at a bus stop with capacity for two people, but the bench is fully exposed to the street and pavement, has no intervening table, and the two seat slots are not differentiated — reads as unfocused-encounter waiting seating (L42 / L57), not as a dyad-supporting nook.
5. A single armchair in a private alcove with no companion seat — single-seat scenes cannot satisfy a constitutively *dyadic* affordance; this is at most an L43 (approach invitation) or solitary-retreat configuration.
6. A pair of bar stools side-by-side along a long straight bar facing the back-bar mirror, separated by 30 inches centre-to-centre, no intervening corner or angle — the parallel orientation and lack of mutual sight-line produce a side-by-side configuration without the partial enclosure that distinguishes a dyadic-intimacy affordance from generic two-seat companion seating.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)

- Paired-seat arrangements where centre-to-centre seat separation falls in Hall's intimate, personal-close, or personal-far zones (i.e., ≤ 122 cm) AND at least one form of partial lateral or rear enclosure is present (booth back, alcove walls, screen, planter, high chair-back, banquette curve, corner geometry).
- Two-slot bench or banquette arrangements where the seating is undivided but the slot-positions are implied by table geometry, place settings, or symmetric cushioning, provided the distance and enclosure conditions are met.
- L-arrangement dyads at corners (room corners, L-shaped counters, L-shaped sectional sofas) where the corner geometry itself provides the partial enclosure even without an additional screening element.
- Side-by-side dyads with intervening shared focal artefact (small table, shared work surface, low partition between seats) and partial rear enclosure, classified as `side_by_side` with a moderate (not maximal) secondary affordance score per Hall's (1966, ch. 10) note that this is the dominant Japanese dyadic-intimacy form.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)

- Single-seat scenes — L48 is constitutively a *two-seat* (or two-slot) construct.
- Multi-party (3+) F-formation seating, even at intimate distances; that is the L44 (sociopetal seating topology) or L49 (small-group arrangement) construct, not L48. A four-chair round-table at intimate-close distance is L49, not L48 with three excess chairs.
- Two-seat arrangements at social-close distance or greater (≥ 122 cm centre-to-centre) regardless of orientation; the distance defeats the *intimacy* component even if the dyadic-seat-count component is satisfied.
- Two-seat arrangements without any partial enclosure — open-floor pairs of chairs facing each other in the middle of a large room may afford dyadic *conversation* (and thus contribute to L44), but the absence of screening means they do not afford dyadic *intimacy* in Hall's or Goffman's sense, where intimacy presupposes some withdrawal from the surrounding civil-inattention field (Goffman, 1971, ch. 2).
- Two-seat presentation arrangements (interviewer/interviewee desks, confessional booths from the priest's side, customer-service counters) where the geometry is dyadic but the interactional ecology is role-asymmetric service rather than mutual dyadic intimacy; these belong to L46 (hosting/service script).

The `none` enum value within L48 itself absorbs the local negation; an explicit inverse tag is logged as a Sprint-2 candidate.

### value_type assignment

- `value_type`: `categorical`
- `value_range`: enum `[vis_a_vis, l_arrangement, side_by_side, semicircular, circular, none]`
- `unit`: `enum`
- `secondary_value_type`: `ordinal`
- `secondary_value_range`: `[0, 4]`
- `secondary_unit`: `likert_0_4`

The categorical primary value names the *kind of dyadic arrangement* implied by the seating geometry (Kendon, 1990, ch. 7). The secondary ordinal carries the *strength of dyadic-intimacy affordance* — a joint function of (a) seat-separation distance against Hall's zones, (b) presence and quality of partial enclosure, and (c) precision of mutual orientation. The two are independent: a `vis_a_vis` dyad at personal-close distance in a booth scores `vis_a_vis` / 4; the same dyad at social-close distance with no screening scores `vis_a_vis` / 1. The `semicircular` and `circular` values are admitted in the enum for schema consistency with L44/L49 but will rarely fire for L48 because two seats cannot enclose a true semicircle or circle without an additional implied focal artefact; when they do fire, it is for tight curved banquettes where two persons sit on a partial arc.

### extraction_notes_2d

The extractor consumes upstream observable tags — most centrally `furniture.chair_instances` (per-chair bounding boxes and instance masks), `furniture.paired_seat_cluster` (two-seat groupings: booths, banquettes, paired armchairs, bench dyads), `furniture.chair_orientation` (per-chair front-normal vector in image plane or its monocular 3D lift), `furniture.seat_separation_distance_estimate` (centre-to-centre distance in metres, monocular-3D-lifted with explicit confidence interval), `furniture.intervening_small_artefact_present` (small tables, sill-tables, chess tables — distinct from the L44 generic "intervening artefact" by a size threshold of < 1 m maximal horizontal extent), `enclosure.partial_screen_present` (booth-back, alcove-wall, planter, high-chair-back, drape, corner geometry), and `scene.floor_plane_normal`. The V2.6 cue list "two-seat nooks; booth seating; side alcoves; partial screening" describes the *furniture configuration* but is severely under-specified on the proxemic component: a two-seat nook with seats at 16 in centre-to-centre affords intimacy (Hall, 1966 — intimate distance 0–46 cm), the same two seats at 44 in centre-to-centre afford a job interview (personal-far). The extractor MUST therefore emit a `distance_zone_estimate` field taking one of `{intimate, personal_close, personal_far, social_close, social_far, public}` per Hall (1966) and panel disposition §3 viii — without this field the categorical and Likert outputs are not interpretable. Predominant-arrangement classification proceeds: 2 chairs at front-normal angle ≈ 180° → `vis_a_vis`; 2 chairs at ≈ 90° → `l_arrangement`; 2 chairs at ≈ 0° (parallel) with shared focal artefact → `side_by_side`; 2 chairs on a tight curved banquette spanning < 180° of arc around an implied focal point → `semicircular`; rare full-arc tight pair → `circular`; otherwise → `none`. The F-formation-geometry sub-field `(o_space_centroid_xy, o_space_radius_px, predominant_arrangement)` is also mandatory output (panel disposition §3 ix). Secondary Likert combines distance-zone + screening-quality + orientation-precision into a 0–4 ordinal: distance in {intimate, personal_close, personal_far} contributes up to +2; one-or-more sides of partial screening contributes up to +1; precise mutual orientation (within 15° of the canonical arrangement angle) contributes up to +1. **Cross-cultural-variance warning (Hall, 1966, ch. 10)**: Hall's distance zones are calibrated on North-American adult middle-class samples. The same physical centre-to-centre separation is read as intimate by North Europeans, as personal by Mediterranean and Latin-American observers, and as personal-far by some East-Asian observers. A Japanese tea-room dyad seated tatami-style at side-by-side with shared focus may occupy a zone that the Western-calibrated extractor classifies as `personal_close` while in its cultural context it is the canonical dyadic-intimacy form. Downstream model trainers MUST treat L48 outputs as culturally indexed and the secondary Likert as conditionally interpretable on the cultural prior of the depicted scene. The Western-calibration warning is non-optional and must be surfaced in any consumer documentation.

### extraction_notes_3d

In a 3D / VR substrate the extraction is materially easier and the under-specification problem in 2D largely disappears: chair instances, orientations, and seat-centre-to-seat-centre distances are all directly metric, the floor plane is given, and partial-enclosure surfaces (booth backs, alcove walls, screens) are directly classifiable from the surrounding mesh geometry. The extractor should take the 3D scene graph, identify candidate paired-seat clusters by spatial proximity (centre-to-centre ≤ 122 cm) and cluster size (exactly 2), compute the seat-front-normal pair angle to assign a categorical-arrangement value, measure the intra-pair distance in metres and bin against Hall's zones to produce `distance_zone_estimate`, then sweep an isovist or visibility region from the dyad-midpoint to count occluding partial-screen surfaces within a 2 m radius for the screening component of the secondary Likert. The o-space centroid is the midpoint of the two seat centres projected onto the floor plane; the o-space radius is half the seat-centre-to-seat-centre distance in metres. In VR, occupancy state (whether avatars are seated) is observable, but L48 is an *affordance* construct and so should be computed from the seating geometry and surrounding enclosure alone, with avatar occupancy contributing only to a separate confidence-of-realisation field. The `requires_floor_plan: false` setting holds even in 3D: L48 is a local two-seat-cluster property with local enclosure, not a configurational property of the room or building.

## Notes

### interaction_mode

`focused`. Dyadic intimacy support is constitutively an arrangement that *invites and supports a focused dyadic encounter* in Goffman's (1963) sense — a sustained mutual involvement around a shared concern between exactly two persons. The construct does not apply to unfocused-encounter spaces (waiting areas, transit benches) and is not mixed-mode: an unfocused two-seat arrangement is, by definition, not a dyadic-intimacy affordance.

### cross_cultural_variance

`high`. Hall (1966, ch. 10) documents marked differences across North American, North European, Mediterranean, Latin American, and East-Asian samples in the centre-to-centre seat separation that subjects judge as comfortably intimate, personal, or social. An extractor calibrated on Western indoor-furniture corpora will systematically (a) over-classify Mediterranean and Latin-American close-angle close-distance dyads as `intimate` when in their cultural context they are personal-close, (b) under-classify Japanese side-by-side-around-shared-focus dyads as low-affordance when they are the canonical dyadic-intimacy form, and (c) misread Middle-Eastern majlis perimeter dyads at distances that exceed the personal-far threshold but remain culturally intimate. Downstream model trainers MUST treat L48 outputs as culturally indexed; the secondary Likert is conditionally interpretable on the cultural prior of the depicted scene. See `extraction_notes_2d` for the explicit Western-calibration warning.
