## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Ease with which a group of two or more can claim a shared spot as theirs.

### definition_long
Group Territorial Claimability is the affordance a setting provides for a co-located group of two or more persons to convert a region of *public* territory into a *secondary* territory that is socially recognised as theirs for the duration of the encounter (Altman, 1975, ch. 6; Goffman, 1971, ch. 2). Operationally, the construct measures the legibility, defensibility, and markability of a candidate spot — the presence of bounding furniture, the visual and physical separation of the spot from circulation flow, and the availability of artefactual surfaces on which the group can deposit *territorial markers* (coats, bags, drinks, programmes) that signal claim to subsequent passers-by. The construct is *configurational at the spot scale* (it is about a specific seat-cluster or alcove, not the whole room) but *cultural at the marker scale* (which artefacts read as a claim varies sharply by setting; Hall, 1966, ch. 10). High claimability means a group can both *establish* and *defend* possession with minimal social work; low claimability means each member must remain present and attentive to retain the spot.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A four-person booth in a diner: padded high backs on three sides, a fixed table, coat hooks at the booth entrance — a group of four arriving and dropping coats and bags signals an unambiguous claim that survives a trip to the restroom.
2. A round corner table in a pub with a built-in bench seat against two walls and a hat rack on the adjacent post; a group of three leaves half-finished pints and a folded newspaper, returns ten minutes later and finds the spot still theirs.
3. A library group-study room with a glass door, a single internal table for six, an interior coat rack, and a whiteboard that the group has begun to fill with writing.
4. A grouping of armchairs around a low table in a hotel lobby, set inside a low planter-bounded alcove off the main circulation path; the planters mark the boundary and the table holds drinks and a laptop bag.
5. A picnic table in a park pavilion with a roof, side benches, and a clear edge between the gravel pad and the surrounding grass; a family lays out a tablecloth and food and the spot is unambiguously theirs.
6. A cafe banquette running the length of one wall, with a row of small tables paired with single chairs opposite; a group of three has pushed two tables together, draped jackets across the chairs, and arranged laptops — the geometry plus the artefactual deposit reads as claim.
7. A conference foyer with clusters of four stackable chairs around small high-tops, each high-top set behind a freestanding panel that breaks line-of-sight from the main aisle; a working group has commandeered one cluster for a side-meeting.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A long communal table in a co-working cafe with no seat backs, no internal subdivisions, and no nearby surfaces for coat-hanging; even a group of five seated together cannot defend the spot if any one of them stands up — the geometry does not support a group claim, only individual seat-occupancy claims.
2. A pair seated knee-to-knee on a small two-top in the centre of an open floor — this is L48 (dyadic intimacy) territory: the configuration affords *intimate dyadic* talk but does not afford a *group* claim of the surrounding region.
3. A bench in a public square with no back, no flanking elements, and a busy through-route on both sides; two people sitting on it cannot claim the bench in any meaningful way beyond the moment of sitting.
4. A row of theatre seats during intermission: each seat is individually claimable by leaving a programme on it, but the *row segment* is not a group-claimable spot — there is no bounded enclosure, no shared surface, and no marker affordance for the group as such.
5. An empty conference room with movable chairs scattered loosely around a long table; the room affords *being assigned* to a group by an external authority but does not, by its geometry alone, afford spontaneous group claim.
6. A bar rail with stools facing the bar — even a group of four standing or seated together cannot claim a "spot" because the geometry is linear, frontally oriented, and offers no enclosure or shared markable surface.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Group sizes of 2 through approximately 8 (above 8 the construct shades into L51 group-assembly affordance and the marker-as-claim mechanism breaks down).
- Both *seated* group claims (booths, banquettes, table clusters) and *standing* group claims (alcoves, panelled foyer pockets, pavilion stages) where the geometry plus artefactual deposit can convert public to secondary territory.
- Indoor and outdoor settings, provided the candidate spot has a definable boundary against the surrounding circulation field.
- Settings in which the claim is *temporary and informal* (the canonical Altman secondary-territory case): the lease is for the duration of the encounter, not by reservation or rental.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- *Primary* territories in Altman's (1975) sense: rented private rooms, reserved tables, ticketed seats — the affordance there is established by external contract, not by the geometry's support for spontaneous claim. L55 is about the *spontaneous-claim* margin.
- Individual-territory claimability (one person leaving a coat on a chair); this is the territories-of-self case (Goffman, 1971, ch. 2) and belongs in a future single-person-claim tag, not L55.
- *Dyadic intimacy* settings where two people are physically close but not claiming the surrounding region as theirs — this is L48 (sociopetal vis-à-vis seating) territory.
- Settings in which the candidate "spot" is so large or so undefined that the question of group claim is incoherent — e.g., the entire floor of a ballroom, an open lawn with no boundary features.
- Wholly *configurational* questions about whether a building affords group co-presence at all — that is L41 (chance-encounter potential) and L51 (group-assembly affordance) territory; L55 presupposes the spot exists and asks how easily a group can lay claim to it.

### factor_associations
- V2.6 indicator structure (inferred from the Observable 2D Cues column): two co-loading sub-factors expected — (a) *spot enclosure* (bounding furniture: booth backs, alcoves, corners, partitions) and (b) *marker affordance* (surfaces and fixtures on which groups deposit territorial markers: tables, coat hooks, shelves, hat racks). The construct is the joint affordance, but the two sub-factors load on a single higher-order factor at the spot level. Phase F.5 should test whether the two sub-factors warrant separate tags.

### related_tags (≥ 2 siblings from L41–L58)
- `social.group_assembly_affordance` (L51) — adjacent at the upper end of group size; L51 covers 7+ assemblies, L55 covers 2–8 spot claims
- `social.boundary_permeability` (L54) — adjacent on the boundary-marker dimension; L54 measures permeability of a region's outer boundary, L55 measures how readily a group can establish such a boundary at a candidate spot
- `social.sociopetal_seating_topology` (L48) — explicitly contrasted: L48 is about intimacy of dyadic conversation given seating geometry; L55 is about group-scale spot claim
- `social.mingling_affordance` (L56) — inverse-related: high mingling affordance is partly the *absence* of strongly claimable spots, since mingling presupposes circulation rather than entrenchment

### inverse_of
`social.mingling_affordance` (partial inverse — high group territoriality and high mingling affordance are typically negatively correlated within a setting, though not strictly mutually exclusive)

### extraction_notes_2d
Extraction proceeds at the *candidate-spot* level rather than the whole-image level: the extractor must first propose one or more candidate spots (e.g., a booth, an alcove, a table cluster) and then score each. Required upstream observables are (a) `affordance.furniture_class_map` to identify booth backs, banquettes, partitions, planters, and other bounding elements; (b) `affordance.surface_inventory` to count tables, shelves, coat hooks, hat racks, and other markable surfaces within the candidate spot; (c) `affordance.bounding_count` — the count of physical or visual boundaries on the four cardinal sides of the candidate spot (a booth scores 3, a corner table scores 2, a centre-floor table scores 0); (d) `affordance.circulation_distance_px` — pixel distance from the candidate spot to the nearest detected circulation path, since spots embedded in circulation are less claimable; (e) `affordance.marker_presence` — detection of already-deposited markers (coats on chairs, bags on tables, drinks left at unoccupied seats), which is direct evidence of claim having been established. The Likert score combines bounding (40%), marker affordance (30%), circulation separation (20%), and observed marker presence (10%). **Western-calibration warning (Hall, 1966, ch. 10):** the marker-affordance and observed-marker components are culturally indexed. Coats-on-chairs, half-finished drinks, and folded newspapers read as territorial claims in English-pub and North-American-diner settings but read differently in a Tokyo izakaya (where shoe placement, oshibori position, and explicit verbal claim by staff bear more of the marker work) or in a Cairo coffeehouse (where the shisha apparatus and a placed cup are stronger markers than personal effects). Extractor outputs from non-Western settings should be flagged with a `cultural_calibration_caveat` and downstream consumers should treat the score as a proposal for human verification rather than a stable measurement. Phase E will require the implementer to emit a `setting_cultural_class` proxy alongside the score so calibration can be applied downstream.

### extraction_notes_3d
With 3D/VR substrate, the bounding component improves substantially: an isovist computed at standing height from the candidate spot's centroid yields a defensibility measure (lower isovist area at the spot height = more enclosed = more claimable, up to a point), and explicit geometric measurement of seat-back height and partition height around the spot directly yields the bounding strength. The marker-surface inventory transitions from class-segmentation guess to explicit object enumeration with surface-area measurement. Circulation-path estimation also improves: pedestrian-flow simulation through the 3D model yields a `circulation_proximity` measure that 2D extraction can only approximate. The cultural-calibration caveat persists: 3D geometry is culture-neutral but the *interpretation* of which artefacts count as territorial markers remains culturally indexed. For VR scenes specifically, consider also the *temporal dynamic* unavailable in stills: how rapidly can a group establish claim (time-to-first-marker) is a behaviorally meaningful supplement to the affordance score and should be considered in Sprint-2 VR extractor design.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
**Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?**

Forgetting the definition_short and definition_long: reading the seven positive examples and six negative examples cold —

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: The positive examples all involve (a) a small-to-medium group (2–7 people), (b) a bounded sub-region within a larger public space, where the boundary is supplied by furniture (booth backs, planters, panels, alcove walls, banquettes), and (c) the deposit of personal artefacts (coats, drinks, bags, laptops, programmes, newspapers, food) that signal the spot is taken. The negative examples are diagnostic: long communal table (no boundary), centre-floor two-top (no group, only dyad), backless bench (no boundary, no surface), theatre row (no shared bounded sub-region), empty room with scattered chairs (no claim yet, no markers), bar rail (linear, no enclosure). The construct appears to be: *the affordance for a group to mark a sub-region of a public space as collectively theirs for the duration of an encounter, via boundary geometry and artefactual deposit*.

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: Converged. The Pass 1 definition_long emphasises (i) Altman's primary/secondary/public distinction, (ii) the spot-scale configurational character, (iii) the cultural variability of the marker mechanism, and (iv) defensibility-without-presence. The blind read recovered (i) the public-to-claimed transition, (ii) the spot-scale character (sub-region within a larger space), and (iii) the marker-and-boundary joint mechanism. The blind read did NOT explicitly recover (iv) defensibility-without-presence (e.g., the booth example mentions a trip to the restroom; the pub example mentions returning ten minutes later — these should be enough). On re-reading the examples, defensibility-without-presence is in fact carried by example 1 ("survives a trip to the restroom") and example 2 ("returns ten minutes later and finds the spot still theirs"). It also did not explicitly recover the cultural variance, but the examples are deliberately drawn from English/American-pub/diner/cafe/library settings, which is consistent with the construct being culturally indexed.

**Action taken**: None. Convergence is sufficient. The defensibility-without-presence component is implicit in two examples; the cultural variance is captured in `extraction_notes_2d` and `notes.cross_cultural_variance` but is appropriately *not* foregrounded in the examples (which would be confusing — examples should illustrate the *Western* canonical case and the cultural caveat lives in extraction notes).
