## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short

Seating geometry whose angular convergence affords sustained face-to-face talk (F-formations).

### definition_long

The spatial-organisational property of a multi-seat arrangement whose chair positions and orientations enclose a shared inner void — Kendon's *o-space* — such that two or more co-present persons, were they to occupy the seats, would form a transactional segment supporting sustained mutual orientation, gaze, and talk (Kendon, 1990, ch. 7). The construct is Osmond's (1957) "sociopetal" pole — arrangements that *bring people together* for conversation — operationalised through the F-formation tradition's distinction between p-space (the slot the bodies and chairs occupy) and o-space (the shared inner region around which the bodies are oriented). It is a property of *arrangements of multiple seats*, not of single chairs; a single chair, however oriented, cannot constitute a sociopetal topology because no o-space is enclosed.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)

1. A pair of armchairs angled inward at roughly 90° across a small coffee table in a hotel lobby; the chair fronts converge on a shared point above the table surface, enclosing a clear o-space.
2. Four café chairs around a small round table, each chair-front normal pointing approximately at the table centre; the inward-facing seat ring defines a circular F-formation.
3. A semicircular cluster of low lounge chairs around a fireplace in a reading room, all chair backs on the convex side and chair fronts converging on the hearth-as-shared-focus.
4. A pair of bench seats in a railway compartment facing each other across a fold-down table, the backs flush with opposite walls and the o-space the table-top void between them — a vis-à-vis arrangement.
5. Three chairs in an L-arrangement at the corner of a sectional sofa, two side-by-side and one perpendicular, all three chair-fronts converging on a coffee-table o-space.
6. A circle of chairs in a therapy-group room with no central table, the o-space defined purely by the convergence of chair-front normals on the empty floor centre.
7. A pair of bar stools turned inward toward each other across the corner of an L-shaped bar counter, fronts converging at the corner.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)

1. A row of theatre seats all facing forward toward a stage — each seat is occupied for focused attention, but the seats face *parallel*, not convergent, so no inter-seat o-space is enclosed; this is a one-to-many presentation arrangement (L52), not sociopetal.
2. A single armchair angled toward an open doorway in a hallway — looks "inviting" because of orientation, but no second seat is present to enclose an o-space; this is at most an approach-invitation cue (L43), not a sociopetal topology.
3. Four airport-terminal chairs welded back-to-back in a cross pattern with all chair-fronts pointing radially *outward* — Osmond's classic sociofugal configuration; the inverse construct.
4. A long boardroom table with chairs only along one side, all facing a wall-mounted screen — chairs face a common focus but do not face *each other*, so no F-formation o-space is enclosed.
5. A pair of chairs side-by-side on a porch, both facing the same garden view — Hall (1966) notes that side-by-side parallel seating affords companionship but not the mutual gaze of vis-à-vis sociopetal seating; classification depends on whether a shared o-space (e.g., a small table between them) is enclosed (see scope_includes 3).
6. A single sofa with three cushions, all facing the same TV — the cushions are clustered but their fronts are coplanar; no convergence, no o-space.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)

- Multi-chair arrangements where chair-front normals converge on a shared point or compact region within roughly 3 metres of all participating seats (the F-formation o-space radius constraint).
- Arrangements where the o-space is enclosed by the *combination* of seats and a shared focal artefact (table, hearth, screen) rather than by the seats alone, provided at least two seats face inward toward that artefact at angles less than 180° apart.
- Side-by-side parallel seating with a small intervening table or shared focal object that re-establishes a partial o-space; classified as the `side_by_side` enum value with a moderate (not maximal) secondary affordance score, per Hall's (1966, ch. 10) observation that this arrangement is the dominant Japanese sociopetal form.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)

- Single-seat scenes regardless of orientation; sociopetal topology is constitutively a *multi-seat* property.
- Parallel-rank seating arrangements (theatre, lecture-hall, transit-bench rows) where all chair-fronts are coplanar and converge only at infinity; these are L52 (presentation) configurations, not sociopetal.
- Sociofugal arrangements (airport terminal back-to-back, library carrel rows facing walls) — these are the *inverse* construct and should receive the `none` enum value with secondary score 0, not a low sociopetal score.
- Standing-only F-formations without seats; these are within the F-formation tradition but outside the *seating* scope of L44 (and largely covered by L56 mingling affordance).
- Workstation/cubicle seating where chairs face screens or work surfaces independently; the convergence is on individual tasks, not on a shared o-space.

### value_type assignment

- `value_type`: `categorical`
- `value_range`: enum `[vis_a_vis, l_arrangement, side_by_side, semicircular, circular, none]`
- `unit`: `enum`
- `secondary_value_type`: `ordinal`
- `secondary_value_range`: `[0, 4]`
- `secondary_unit`: `likert_0_4`

The categorical primary value names the *kind of arrangement implied by the seating geometry* (Kendon 1990, ch. 7). The secondary ordinal carries the *strength of affordance* judgment — i.e., for a recognised vis-à-vis arrangement, how strongly the chair-distance, intervening-artefact configuration, and orientation-precision afford sustained focused conversation. The two are independent: a vis-à-vis arrangement at 4 m distance with a giant table between participants is `vis_a_vis` with secondary score 1; the same arrangement at conversational distance with a small coffee table is `vis_a_vis` with secondary score 4.

### extraction_notes_2d

The extractor consumes upstream observable tags — most centrally `furniture.chair_instances` (per-chair bounding box and instance mask), `furniture.chair_orientation` (per-chair front-normal vector in image plane, or its monocular 3D lift), `furniture.table_instances` and `furniture.hearth_instances` (intervening-artefact candidates), and `scene.floor_plane_normal` (for projecting chair fronts onto the floor plane to get planar angular geometry). The Setti et al. (2013) detection pipeline is the closest published precedent: detect upper-body or chair-front orientations, then cluster orientation lines by their intersection points to identify candidate F-formation o-spaces. For L44 specifically, the extractor MUST emit, alongside the categorical and secondary-Likert values, the F-formation-geometry sub-field `(o_space_centroid_xy, o_space_radius_px, predominant_arrangement)` per Kendon-panel mandate (Kendon, 1990, pp. 209–214); the V2.6 cue list "seats facing each other; circular/semicircular arrangements; clustered chairs" describes only the p-space furniture and elides the o-space, which is the analytically-load-bearing region. Predominant-arrangement classification proceeds: 2 chairs at front-normal angle ~180° → `vis_a_vis`; 2 chairs at ~90° → `l_arrangement`; 2+ chairs at ~0° (parallel) with shared focal artefact → `side_by_side`; 3+ chairs whose front-normals converge on a common centroid with chair-back arc spanning <180° of the centroid → `semicircular`; same with arc spanning ≥270° → `circular`; otherwise → `none`. Distance-zone estimate per Hall (1966) is required as a separate extractor-output field (panel disposition §3 viii). **Cross-cultural-variance warning (Hall, 1966, ch. 10)**: this extractor is calibrated against Western indoor-furniture corpora; it will systematically misread (a) Japanese tea-room and tatami arrangements where side-by-side around a shared focus substitutes for face-to-face, (b) Mediterranean/Latin-American close-angle conversational seating which the angular threshold may classify as `none` because the chairs are too close to resolve a stable o-space, and (c) Middle-Eastern majlis perimeter seating which is technically circular but at distances that exceed the o-space radius constraint. Downstream model trainers MUST treat L44 outputs as culturally indexed and the secondary Likert as conditionally interpretable on the cultural prior of the depicted scene.

### extraction_notes_3d

In a 3D / VR substrate the extraction is materially easier: chair instances and orientations are already metric, the floor plane is given, and the F-formation o-space is computable as the smallest enclosed region whose boundary contains all chair-front rays within their first 2.5 m. The extractor should take the 3D scene graph, project chair-front normals onto the floor plane, compute pairwise ray intersections (or Voronoi-style closest-approach centroids for parallel-front cases), cluster the intersections by spatial proximity to identify candidate o-space centroids, and emit the largest cluster's centroid as `o_space_centroid_xy` and its enclosing-circle radius as `o_space_radius_m` (note: metric in 3D, pixels in 2D). The arrangement-enum classification applies the same angular thresholds as in 2D but with metric distance gating: vis-à-vis at >3 m intra-chair separation drops the secondary affordance score by 1 step per metre. In VR, occupancy state (whether the avatars are actually present) is observable, but L44 is an *affordance* construct and so should be computed from the seating geometry alone, with avatar occupancy contributing only to a separate confidence-of-realisation field. The `requires_floor_plan: false` setting holds even in 3D: L44 is a local seat-cluster property, not a configurational property of the room.

## Notes

### interaction_mode

`focused`. Sociopetal seating is constitutively an arrangement that *invites and supports a focused encounter* in Goffman's (1963) sense — a sustained mutual involvement around a shared concern. The construct does not apply to unfocused-encounter spaces (waiting areas with sociofugal seating) and is mixed-mode only at the boundary case of side-by-side arrangements with a partial shared focus.

### cross_cultural_variance

`high`. Hall (1966, ch. 10) documents marked differences across North American, Mediterranean, and Japanese sociopetal conventions; an extractor calibrated on Western corpora will systematically mis-classify Japanese side-by-side-around-shared-focus arrangements as `side_by_side` with a low secondary affordance score, when in their cultural context they are the maximally sociopetal form. See `extraction_notes_2d` for the explicit Western-calibration warning.

### extraction_notes

No V2.6 Tier override applied — L44 remains Tier 1 as in V2.6. Two extractor-contract requirements deserve special note for the Phase E stub author: (a) the F-formation-geometry sub-field `(o_space_centroid_xy, o_space_radius_px, predominant_arrangement)` is mandatory output (Kendon panel mandate, panel disposition §3 ix); (b) the Hall distance-zone estimate per Hall (1966) is mandatory output (Hall panel mandate, panel disposition §3 viii). Both are runtime extractor-output specifications, not registry-level fields, and so are recorded here rather than in `extractability`.
