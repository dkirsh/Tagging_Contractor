## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Triadic anchor: salient focal object plus seating oriented toward it, fixing shared focus.

### definition_long
A *shared attention anchor* is the spatial substrate of the joint-attention triad — the configuration in which a salient environmental feature (a focal object, wall, display, screen, hearth, central table feature) is paired with seating, postural, or circulation orientations that converge on that feature, jointly fixing a stable shared focus for the gathering. The construct is binary at first approximation: either the room commits to a single, materially-marked anchor that the seating arrangement actively orients toward, or it does not. The triadic specification is constitutive, not optional. Following Tomasello (1995, 2008), joint attention requires the simultaneous coordination of self, other, and a shared object of attention; the spatial-registry analog therefore requires both (i) a perceptually dominant anchor and (ii) a seating or body-axis arrangement that orients the participants' gaze and posture toward it. Following Kendon (1990, ch. 7), the anchor functions as the shared *focus* of the o-space — the inner transactional region of an F-formation — and a focal feature without an o-space oriented toward it is not a shared attention anchor but merely a salient object. Following Goffman (1963, ch. 6), a focused gathering is constituted by the participants' coordinated involvement in a single cognitive and visual concern; the anchor is the material correlate of that concern. L53 thus differs from L52 (one-to-many presentation) in that L52 specifies the *participation framework* (asymmetric speaker/audience) whereas L53 specifies whether a *triadic anchor* exists — a hearth-and-circle, a central conference table feature, or a museum display with a viewing bench all qualify for L53 without being one-to-many presentations.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A living room with a fireplace and a U-shaped sofa-and-armchair arrangement whose seating axes all converge on the hearth — the fireplace is the materially-marked anchor and the seating commits the room to it.
2. A museum gallery with a single sculpture on a central plinth and a curved bench arc placed at the canonical viewing distance facing the plinth.
3. A boardroom with an oblong conference table whose centre carries a fixed visual feature (a built-in display, an architectural model, a centrepiece) and chairs arrayed around the table with their long axes oriented toward that centre.
4. A campfire-style outdoor gathering: a fire ring at the centre, log benches arranged in a ring, every body axis oriented inward and downward toward the flames.
5. A waiting area in a children's hospital with a fish tank along one wall and a row of seats positioned and angled to face the tank, such that the tank functions as a shared point of regard for waiting families.
6. A classroom corner reading nook organised around a low display shelf of picture books, with floor cushions arrayed in a semicircle facing the shelf.
7. A traditional Japanese tea room with a *tokonoma* alcove containing a hanging scroll and arranged flowers; the seating positions for guests are fixed by convention to face the alcove, making it the canonical shared focus.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A blank wall in a hotel lobby with a single piece of large abstract art mounted on it, but the lobby seating is arranged around small coffee tables with seats facing each other rather than the wall — the focal object is present but no triadic anchor exists. *(Per the panel: focal object without seating oriented toward it is mere object presence, not a shared attention anchor.)*
2. A fast-food restaurant with a wall-mounted television playing news, but tables and chairs are arranged for paired or quartet conversation with seats facing across the table; the television is salient but the seating refuses it as an anchor.
3. A hotel lobby with multiple competing focal candidates — a reception desk, a grand staircase, a video wall, a fountain — and dispersed seating clusters that orient toward different candidates or toward each other; no single shared anchor is established.
4. An airport gate area with rows of perimeter seating facing a central empty floor; there is no central feature and the seating axis converges on nothing.
5. A bare meeting room with chairs arranged in a circle around an empty conversation centre; the o-space exists (sociopetal seating) but there is no materially-marked anchor occupying it — this is L48/L49 territory, not L53.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Domestic hearth-and-seating arrangements (fireplaces, central low tables, family altars) where the anchor is a permanent architectural or furniture feature and the surrounding seating commits the room to it.
- Display-zone configurations (museum benches before sculpture, gallery viewing seats before paintings, observatory seating before a window or telescope) where the anchor is curatorially designated and seating is calibrated to it.
- One-to-many presentation rooms (lecture halls, classrooms) where L52 *and* L53 both apply: the front region is also a shared attention anchor, and the audience seating orientation simultaneously satisfies both constructs. (L53 is not exclusive of L52; the constructs co-occur frequently. See `related_tags`.)
- Central-table configurations whose table-top itself carries a focal feature (architectural model, game board, candelabrum, food platter at a banquet) that anchors the gaze of the seated participants inward and downward.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- **Focal object without seating oriented toward it.** Mere object presence — a striking wall, a large artwork, a display screen — does not constitute a shared attention anchor unless the seating arrangement actively orients toward it. *(Panel-mandated exclusion: per Kendon, the V2.6 cue "dominant focal object/wall" identifies a candidate anchor but not the triadic structure that joint attention requires. A focal wall with seats facing it constitutes a triad; a focal wall with seats facing each other does not.)*
- **Sociopetal seating without a materially-marked anchor.** A circle of chairs around an empty centre is an F-formation o-space (L48 / L49 / L51) but lacks the object pole of the joint-attention triad and is therefore not L53.
- **Multi-anchor or competing-anchor configurations.** Rooms with several plausible focal candidates and dispersed orientations do not commit to a single shared focus and should be coded negative on L53 even if individual sub-clusters within the room would qualify on their own.
- **Pure platform-format configurations where the anchor is the speaker rather than an environmental feature.** L52 captures the speaker-and-audience commitment as a *participation framework*; L53 captures the *environmental object* pole of the triad. A bare lectern with no displayed content or backdrop, in a room committed to one-to-many seating, satisfies L52 without satisfying L53. (In practice, most lecture halls satisfy both — the screen, board, or framed front region acts as the L53 anchor while the speaker animates the L52 framework.)
- **Transient or contingent foci** (a person being looked at, a momentary event) that depend on participant behaviour rather than the room's spatial commitments. The registry codes affordances for joint attention, not behavioural episodes of joint attention.

### factor_associations
- V2.6 *Latent_Indicators* sheet, factor "Shared Focus Anchor": rating items for (a) presence of a perceptually dominant focal object/wall/display, (b) presence of a central table feature, (c) presence of a designated display zone, (d) seating orientation convergence on the candidate anchor. The fourth indicator — orientation convergence — is the operationalisation of the triadic-structure requirement. Behavioural proxies (people actually looking at the anchor) belong in extractor docstrings, not the registry.

The constructs frequently co-occur (lecture halls, classrooms, places of worship) but are independently variable (a museum display with a viewing bench is L53 without being L52; a lectern in an empty room is L52 without being L53).

Sociofugal and multi-focus configurations are both L53-negative but for different reasons; folding them into a single inverse construct would conflate them.)

### extraction_notes_2d
A 2D image is sufficient when upstream observables jointly identify (a) a single dominant anchor candidate AND (b) seating whose orientation axes converge on that candidate. The extractor should consume upstream tags `affordance.focal_object_present` (binary on whether a perceptually dominant non-seating feature exists — fireplace, sculpture, screen, central table feature, display wall), `affordance.focal_object_saliency_score` (a continuous saliency or visual-prominence score for the top-ranked candidate, used to discriminate single-anchor from competing-anchor scenes), `affordance.focal_object_polygon` (region support for the anchor itself), `furniture.chair_orientation_field` (per-chair yaw vector), `furniture.seating_centroid` (the geometric centre of the seated region), and `geometry.anchor_to_seating_axis_angle` (the angle between the seating-orientation principal direction and the anchor-to-centroid vector). The binary positive judgement requires (i) `affordance.focal_object_present == true`, (ii) `affordance.focal_object_saliency_score` above a single-anchor discrimination threshold (i.e. one anchor visually dominates rather than competing with peers), AND (iii) ≥ 60% of detected seating yaw vectors falling within ±35° of the anchor-direction vector from the seating centroid. The orientation-convergence criterion is the operationalisation of the panel-mandated triadic-structure requirement: a focal wall *with* seats facing it constitutes a triad; a focal wall *with seats facing each other* does not, and must be coded negative even when the anchor candidate is visually unambiguous. Because L53 co-occurs with L52 in most presentation contexts, the extractor should NOT short-circuit on L52-positive scenes; instead it should re-verify the anchor-orientation criterion independently, since some L52-positive scenes (a lectern in an empty room, a podium with no backdrop) lack the L53 anchor object even when the participation framework is committed. Cross-cultural variance is medium: the anchor-and-orientation pattern recurs widely (hearths, altars, *tokonoma* alcoves, display niches all exemplify it), but cultures vary in *what kinds of objects* qualify as canonical anchors and in *how strongly* the seating commitment is materially marked versus conventionally enforced. Extractors trained on Western interiors may underweight non-Western anchor types (alcoves, low altars, ground-level focal arrangements) and should be audited cross-culturally before deployment.

### extraction_notes_3d
In 3D / VR scans the same upstream observables are available with stronger geometric grounding. Anchor-candidate detection benefits from full 3D saliency: a fireplace, sculpture, or display zone that is partially occluded in a 2D image is fully recoverable from mesh geometry. Per-chair yaw vectors are recoverable from chair-back normal vectors rather than monocular pose estimation, and the orientation-convergence criterion can be evaluated by testing whether the union of chair viewing frusta intersects coherently within the bounding region of the anchor polygon. The 3D extractor should compute (i) the saliency-ranked anchor candidate set within the visible mesh (using a combination of geometric prominence — surface-area exposure, central placement — and material-marker cues such as luminance or material discontinuity), (ii) per-chair viewing direction from chair-back normals, (iii) the intersection point or region of densest intersection of those view rays, and (iv) whether that intersection region overlaps the top-ranked anchor candidate's bounding region. A 3D positive instance requires the orientation-intersection region to fall within (or immediately adjacent to) the anchor-candidate region. Monocular-3D-approximation extraction is also viable (Tier 1 in V2.6) using monocular depth estimation plus a planar-floor assumption; the binary judgement is robust to depth noise because it depends on the *direction* of seating-axis convergence relative to the anchor rather than absolute distances. Region support is true: positive instances should return both the anchor polygon and the seated-region polygon, allowing downstream consumers to verify the triad geometry. The 3D extractor should also emit (alongside the binary value) the diagnostic triple `(anchor_polygon, seating_centroid, mean_axis_misalignment_degrees)` so that borderline cases can be inspected in Sprint 2 calibration.

### bn_notes
L53 is one of three binary-candidate latents (with L46 and L52) for which the panel's Spohn voice flagged a Sprint-2 ranking-theoretic recoding recommendation. The binary `latent_score` value type is the right Sprint-1 commitment because (i) the construct is genuinely categorical at first approximation — the room either commits to a triadic anchor or it does not — and (ii) the registry currently supports only probabilistic CPTs, and forcing a 5-state ordinal CPT for a near-binary construct would inflate the parameter count without explanatory gain. Spohn (2012, §5.3) argues that constructs of this binary "regime-switch" character are more naturally encoded as ranking functions over disbelief levels, which support default reasoning and graceful degradation under partial evidence. In particular, L53's triadic-structure requirement makes it well-suited to a ranking-theoretic encoding because the construct admits a principled default — *no anchor* is the default disbelief-rank-0 case, and *anchor present* requires positive evidence on both poles of the triad. Sprint 2's BN-integration phase should evaluate whether L53 (and L46, L52) should be migrated from binary `latent_score` to a ranking-theoretic encoding once the registry schema admits ranking-function value types. Until then, the binary value type is honoured but should be regarded as provisional.

The Spohn 50%-overlap rule applies to `bn.parent_tags`. L53's parent set is dominated by `affordance.focal_object_*` observables (the object pole of the triad) and by orientation-related observables (the participant pole). The most likely overlap conflict in audit is with **L52 (one-to-many presentation)**, which shares the orientation-related observables `furniture.chair_orientation_field` and `furniture.seating_row_axis`. The pair is intentionally distinguished by L53's reliance on `affordance.focal_object_*` (a generic salient feature, not necessarily a presentation marker) versus L52's reliance on `affordance.podium_present` / `affordance.screen_or_board_present` / `affordance.lectern_present` (presentation-specific markers). To stay under the 50% overlap bound, L53's parent set is specified to use `geometry.anchor_to_seating_axis_angle` and `pose.head_yaw_field` (gaze observables) rather than the generic `furniture.chair_orientation_field` parent that L52 uses, even though the underlying upstream signal is related. Phase D `audit-extraction-plan` will verify the disjointness of these parent sets; if the 50%-overlap test fails, the resolution is to further decompose `geometry.anchor_to_seating_axis_angle` into anchor-direction-specific observables that L52 does not consume.

A second potential overlap is with the **F-formation cluster (L48 / L49)**, which shares orientation observables. The disambiguating parent is `affordance.focal_object_present`: F-formations specify the o-space's geometric arrangement without requiring a materially-marked anchor within it, while L53 requires both. Audit should treat the F-formation cluster's lack of an `affordance.focal_object_*` parent as the structural distinguisher.

### validation_notes
Pending Phase F.5 panel vote.

*Preserved theoretical scaffolding (recorded for student handoff):* L53 is the spatial-registry analog of Tomasello's joint-attention triad (self–other–object) and Kendon's o-space focus. The construct could in principle be split into two sub-tags — `social.focal_anchor_present` (object pole only) and `social.anchor_orientation_convergence` (orientation pole only) — and the binary L53 reconstructed as their conjunction. The Sprint 1 disposition keeps L53 as a single tag because (i) the V2.6 taxonomy ships it as a single latent and traceability matters within Sprint 1, and (ii) the construct's analytic value derives precisely from the conjunction — the two poles individually are less explanatorily useful than their joint commitment. Students may wish to relitigate this in Week 5: a separation would make the BN structure cleaner (the two poles could become independent observables, with L53 as a derived conjunction) at the cost of an additional registry tag and consumer-contract churn. Also worth flagging: L53 and L52 frequently co-occur but are independently variable, and the registry should not collapse them. A lectern in an empty room is L52-positive and L53-negative; a museum bench before a sculpture is L52-negative and L53-positive; a lecture hall with a screen is both. Maintaining the distinction lets downstream BN consumers reason about the participation framework and the environmental anchor as separate parents of, e.g., engagement or shared-cognition outcomes.
