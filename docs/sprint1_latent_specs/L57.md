## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Spatial-physical conditions facilitating face-saving departure from an encounter.

### definition_long
L57 indexes the degree to which the spatial-physical configuration of a setting *facilitates* — but does not *constitute* — the ritual accomplishment of leave-taking from a focused or unfocused interaction. The construct is anchored in Goffman's (1967, *Interaction Ritual*, pp. 41–45) treatment of departure as a face-saving ceremonial sequence and in Goffman's (1963, *Behavior in Public Places*, pp. 100–107) account of how the surrounding environment supplies (or withholds) the props, exits, and alternate destinations that allow a participant to withdraw without imputing rejection of co-participants. Schegloff and Sacks (1973) established the parallel claim for talk: closing sequences are accomplishments, not events, and they require collaborative scaffolding. L57 measures only the *physical scaffolding* — the presence of multiple egress paths, visible alternate destinations, absence of dead-ends adjacent to seating — that lowers the conversational and ceremonial cost of disengagement. The ritual itself, the farewell display, the tying-off utterances, and the negotiated reading of who-leaves-first are *invisible to the registry*; L57 is a measure of the room, not the parting.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A cafe seating area that opens onto a wide pedestrian aisle, with a counter, a magazine rack, and a doorway all visible from the table — a departing guest can stand, gesture toward any of three plausible next-destinations, and walk out without backing away from the table.
2. An open-plan office breakout zone with seating arranged near a corridor intersection, so that someone leaving a chat can naturally drift toward either the kitchen, the printer, or the corridor without retracing steps past the conversation.
3. A hotel lobby lounge with seating placed in a bay opening onto the main concourse, with visible cues (reception desk, elevator bank, gift shop) that supply socially-credible reasons to rise and depart.
4. A university common room with multiple doorways on different walls and circulation paths between seating clusters, allowing a student to exit one cluster and join, pass, or simply traverse toward another without an awkward U-turn.
5. A reception or vernissage hall with seating perimeter and refreshment stations distributed across the floor, so that withdrawing from one conversation puts the participant immediately into the orbit of a different plausible engagement.
6. A waiting area at a clinic where chairs face a wide circulation aisle leading to the exit, the restroom, and a self-service water station, allowing a patient who has been chatting with a stranger to stand, indicate one of these destinations, and disengage without explanation.
7. A library reading nook positioned at the junction of two main aisles, with stacks visible in multiple directions, so that a researcher can leave a casual exchange by drifting toward any of several shelves.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A deep dining booth at the back of a narrow restaurant, where the only egress path requires squeezing past the seated co-participant — the booth may be intimate, but exit is socially expensive and cannot be performed without explicit, attention-claiming negotiation. (Looks intimate, not high-disengagement-ease.)
2. A pair of armchairs facing each other in a windowless alcove with one doorway directly behind one chair — the participant in the inner chair must rise, walk past or around the other, and traverse a single visible path; there are no alternate destinations to glance toward as a face-saving pretext.
3. A wide-open plaza with abundant exits but no nearby alternate destinations or props — physically easy to leave, but lacking the visible "somewhere I am going" cues that distinguish a credible departure from an abrupt withdrawal. (High egress, low disengagement *ease* in the Goffmanian sense.)
4. A conference panel hall with a single rear doorway: leaving requires walking the length of the room under the eyes of the remaining audience, even though the doorway is unobstructed. (High visibility of exit, low ease of face-saving departure.)
5. A backyard patio with multiple gates but where the host is positioned at the only viable path to each — egress is geometrically multiple but socially singular.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Settings where the participant is engaged in either a focused conversation OR an unfocused co-presence (waiting room, shared bench), provided there is some social cost to a sudden departure.
- Both indoor and outdoor encounter settings (cafes, lobbies, plazas with seating, lounges, common rooms).
- Configurations in which alternate plausible destinations (counters, restrooms, exits, refreshment stations, displays) are *visible from the seated/standing position* of the encounter.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- The ritual sequence of leave-taking itself (farewell utterances, parting displays, hand-shake, hat-tip, reciprocal "good to see you" exchange) — these are conversational/behavioural, not spatial, and L57 does not attempt to measure them. Schegloff & Sacks (1973) on closing sequences applies but is not within L57's measurement domain.
- Pure egress capacity (number and width of exits) divorced from the social geometry of departure — a fire-code-compliant room with many exits but no plausible "next destination" cues scores low on L57 even if it scores high on egress.
- Settings where there is no encounter to disengage from — an empty room cannot be evaluated for disengagement ease except in the conditional sense of "if an encounter occurred here, how easily could it be ended" (acceptable, but should be tagged with a confidence reduction).
- Wayfinding ease for navigation purposes (covered by separate wayfinding tags), as distinct from the social affordance of having credible alternate destinations to *gesture toward*.

### factor_associations
- V2.6 Latent_Indicators: indicators centre on (i) count and visibility of egress paths from the seated position, (ii) presence of alternate visible destinations within the immediate field of view, (iii) absence of dead-end seating geometries (chairs trapped against a wall with no flanking circulation). The V2.6 sheet treats these three indicators as a single composite without explicit factor structure; Sprint 2 may decompose into "egress-path multiplicity" and "alternate-destination visibility" as separable sub-factors.

### related_tags (≥ 2 siblings from L41–L58)
- social.interactional_visibility (L42) — the inverse pole on the engagement/disengagement axis: L42 indexes opportunity to *enter* an encounter through mutual sight; L57 indexes opportunity to *exit* one without ritual cost
- social.boundary_permeability (L54) — both concern movement across a setting's social-spatial boundary, but L54 is about ingress/permeability of the territory while L57 is about egress from an active encounter inside it
- social.chance_encounter_potential (L41) — both are configurational properties of the encounter system, jointly governing the probability and reversibility of unfocused-to-focused-to-unfocused transitions
- social.mingling_affordance (L56) — a high-mingling setting tends to also be high in disengagement ease (alternate destinations are densely available), but the constructs are distinct: L56 measures circulation among multiple potential partners; L57 measures the cost of withdrawing from any one of them

### inverse_of
null — there is no single canonical opposing construct. The closest pole in the registry is the implicit "engagement entrapment" condition flagged in some examples_negative, but this is not a named latent in V2.6 or Sprint 1.

### extraction_notes_2d
L57 is V2.6 Tier 3 and remains so: `from_2d: "no"`. The construct cannot be reliably scored from a single 2D image because it depends jointly on (a) the topology of circulation paths surrounding the encounter region, (b) the presence and visibility — *from the seated position of a co-participant* — of plausible alternate destinations, and (c) the social-physical geometry of the seating itself. While candidate upstream observables exist (notional `affordance.exit_count`, `affordance.alternate_destination_visibility`, `geometry.seating_dead_end_score`, `geometry.circulation_aisle_proximity`), no single-image inference of these is currently validated, and the Goffmanian construct itself sits one level above any geometric primitive: L57 measures *facilitation of a ritual*, and the ritual is invisible. A future Sprint-2 or Sprint-3 effort might assemble a partial proxy from upstream tags `affordance.exit_count`, `geometry.circulation_topology`, and a VLM-scored `vlm.alternate_destination_visibility`, but until those upstream observables are themselves validated and the Goffmanian construct-validity audit is repeated, L57 is non-extractable. Cross-cultural variance is `medium`: the *spatial* facilitation generalises reasonably across Western and East Asian encounter settings, even though the *ritual content* of leave-taking varies substantially.

### extraction_notes_3d
3D / VR substrates do not rescue L57 from Tier 3 in this sprint: `from_3d_vr: "no"` and `monocular_3d_approx: "no"`. A 3D model or VR walkthrough does supply richer inputs — accurate isovist computation from each seat (notional `affordance.isovist_area_per_seat`), reliable circulation-graph extraction (notional `geometry.circulation_graph`), and accurate counting of plausible alternate destinations within each isovist — and in principle a future spec could promote L57 to Tier 2 on 3D substrate alone. The reason for not doing so in Sprint 1 is twofold. First, the construct's dependence on *what counts as a plausible alternate destination* is irreducibly semantic and culturally indexed (a vending machine is plausible in some cultures, a shrine in others), and no validated upstream observable for "plausible destination" yet exists in the registry. Second, the panel mandate (per the L57 special note) is to foreground the construct as a *facilitation* of a ritual that the registry cannot see; promoting extractability before the construct itself is operationalised would create exactly the false-precision risk the panel disposition warns against.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
*Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?*

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: The examples consistently contrast two situations: ones in which a person engaged in a conversation (or co-presence) can credibly stand up and walk away by gesturing toward some other plausible destination — a counter, a restroom, a shelf, a printer, an exit through which they were going anyway — versus ones where leaving requires either physical contortion (squeezing past someone in a booth), a long lonely walk under others' gaze (the conference hall), or an explicit verbal account ("I have to go now"). Negative example 3 is the most informative: it explicitly contrasts physical egress capacity with the *social* affordance of having a credible "I am going somewhere" pretext. Negative example 5 is the most subtle: many gates, but the host's positioning collapses them all to one socially viable path. The inferred construct is therefore: *the degree to which the spatial-physical configuration provides credible face-saving cover for departure from an active encounter*.

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: The Pass 1 definition reads: "the degree to which the spatial-physical configuration of a setting *facilitates* — but does not *constitute* — the ritual accomplishment of leave-taking." The blind-read inference matches this closely: both pick out face-saving cover, both distinguish physical egress from social affordance, both locate the construct in the room rather than in the parting itself. The blind-read inference does not separately surface the Goffmanian "ritual" framing — it arrives at "credible face-saving cover" without naming the ritual — but this is a wording difference rather than a construct divergence, and the negative examples 3 and 5 do the work of separating mere physical egress from socially credible departure.

**Action taken**: None. The examples carry the construct.

## Notes

### interaction_mode
unfocused

The ritual disengagement of a focused encounter is itself the dissolution of focused interaction back into unfocused co-presence; L57's measurement domain is therefore the unfocused-interaction substrate that receives the participant after departure. This follows the panel-mandated assignment in §8 of the field map.

### cross_cultural_variance
medium

The *spatial* facilitation captured by L57 (multiple egress paths, alternate visible destinations, absence of seating dead-ends) generalises reasonably across cultures even though the *ritual content* of leave-taking varies substantially (depth and number of farewell exchanges, hand-shake vs. bow vs. embrace, host-led vs. guest-initiated departure). The construct is therefore not flagged `high` like L43, L44, L48, L49, or L55 — but `medium` rather than `low` because the threshold of what counts as a "plausible alternate destination" is culturally indexed, and downstream model trainers should treat L57 outputs as carrying mild cultural calibration risk.

### extraction_notes
Tier 3 status retained from V2.6 with no override. The panel-mandated framing is documented above in `definition_long` and `extraction_notes_2d`: L57 measures *spatial-physical conditions facilitating* the ritual disengagement, not the ritual itself. The ritual sequence (farewell display, closing tokens per Schegloff & Sacks 1973) is invisible to the registry. No extractor stub will be emitted in Sprint 2 for L57; the entry exists in the registry to (a) reserve the construct's place in the social-interaction layer, (b) document the Goffmanian framing for the student handoff, and (c) anchor the upstream observables that future sprints might assemble into a partial proxy.
