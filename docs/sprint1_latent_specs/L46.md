## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Clarity of who serves whom and where to ask for help in a service-encounter setting.

### definition_long
The degree to which a setting visibly communicates the structure of a service encounter — i.e., the role asymmetry between host/server and guest/client, the locus at which help may be requested, and the procedural sequence the guest is expected to follow upon entry. The construct is binary in epistemic content (the script is either readable from the setting or it is not), following Goffman's account of the service encounter as a *front-region performance* whose intelligibility depends on coordinated sign-vehicles — uniform, position, signage, and furniture — rather than on any single artefact (Goffman, 1959, ch. 3). Schank and Abelson (1977) supply the cognitive correlate: a "restaurant script" or "clinic script" is retrievable from the setting only when the visible cues converge on a single role distribution. Where role asymmetry is undermarked, the visitor cannot enact the script and must either improvise or query a co-present person, both of which are evidence of script *unclarity*.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A medical-clinic waiting area with a clearly elevated reception counter, a sign reading "Please check in here", a visible queue rail, and a uniformed attendant facing the entry.
2. A hotel lobby with a long marked desk, a "Concierge" placard, a uniformed staffer behind it, and a separate signed "Check-in / Check-out" station.
3. A restaurant entry with a host stand placed immediately inside the door, a "Please wait to be seated" sign, and a host in branded attire holding menus.
4. A bank branch with a numbered queue ticket dispenser, a single-file rope-and-stanchion queue, and a row of teller windows facing the queue.
5. A government-services lobby with a triage desk at entry, signage indicating "Information / All visitors first", and visible role-asymmetry between staff (badged, behind desk) and visitors (in front).
6. A boutique with a clearly identified sales counter near the entry, a staff member in a recognisable apron stationed behind it, and a discreet sign reading "Ask us for help finding your size".
7. A library reference area with a marked "Reference Help" desk, a librarian seated behind it, and signage directing patrons to the desk for assistance.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A high-end omakase restaurant with no host stand and no visible counter at entry — the script *is* clear (you wait to be greeted by the chef-host) but is conveyed through cultural convention rather than visible furniture; this is *positive in script clarity but negative in the V2.6 cue set*, and resolution depends on whether the VLM reads the cultural frame.
2. A coworking lounge with a generic reception desk that is unstaffed, has no signage, and no queue rail — desk is present but the script is *not* readable; visitors do not know whether to approach, wait, or self-serve.
3. A hotel lobby with multiple competing counters (concierge, check-in, bell, business centre) all unlabelled and staffed identically — desk-presence is high but role distribution is ambiguous.
4. A retail store entry with no counter visible from the threshold and staff in plain clothes mixing with shoppers — neither sign-vehicle nor role-asymmetry is legible.
5. A continental café where the script (order at table, pay later) is clear *culturally* but the room contains a counter that suggests counter-service — the artefact contradicts the script.
6. A medical practice with a desk that *looks like* reception but is actually for billing only, with no entry-point signage — visitor receives a misleading script.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Settings where role asymmetry is conveyed jointly by furniture (counter, desk, queue rail) AND by signage AND by staff position/attire — convergent multi-cue clarity.
- Desk-less service encounters where the script is conveyed by host position, attire, and entry-point greeting affordance (e.g., a maître d' standing at a podium that is itself a sign-vehicle).
- Self-service settings where the *absence* of a host is itself the script (e.g., signs reading "Please seat yourself" plus visible service-call buttons at tables) and is consistently communicated.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- **Mere presence of a reception desk without role-asymmetry signage.** A desk alone is one sign-vehicle among many; treating its presence as sufficient evidence of script clarity is the category mistake Goffman (1959, ch. 3) warns against. Many service performances are deliberately desk-less (concierge, host, maître d').
- Settings with strong wayfinding signage but no service-encounter component (e.g., a museum with directional signs but no host or service desk) — clarity here pertains to navigation, not to a service script.
- Bureaucratic queue infrastructure that is present but uninhabited (e.g., closed teller windows behind closed shutters at off-hours) — the script is undefined when the role-bearers are absent.
- Living rooms, conference rooms, and other settings whose social purpose is non-service: the construct is undefined where no service encounter is at issue.

### extraction_notes_2d
The binary judgment is produced by a VLM prompt that conditions on the convergence of three upstream observable channels rather than on any single artefact. Required upstream tags (expected names; Phase D will reconcile): `furniture.service_counter_presence` (counter/desk/podium detection), `signage.service_role_text_presence` (OCR of strings such as "Reception", "Check-in", "Please wait to be seated", "Ask for help"), `person.staff_attire_indicator` (uniform/badge/apron classifier with role-asymmetry inference relative to non-staff persons in frame), and `furniture.queue_infrastructure_presence` (rope-and-stanchion, ticket dispenser, marked queue lane). The VLM is prompted to return 1 iff at least two of these channels are positive AND their joint configuration is interpretable as a single coherent service script; otherwise 0. Cross-cultural variance is medium because while desk-presence varies less than proxemics, the *shape* of service scripts varies (Western counter-service vs. Japanese omakase vs. continental waitstaff); the prompt template must list non-Western desk-less script exemplars to avoid Western-calibration bias. Because no clean geometric primitive captures script clarity, geometric extractors alone (oriented-front detection, isovist) are insufficient — the VLM is required.

### extraction_notes_3d
3D / VR substrates supply the same evidence with stronger geometry: depth disambiguates "counter behind which a staff figure is positioned" from "table at which staff and guest sit jointly" (a Goffmanian frame distinction that 2D often collapses). Required upstream tags in 3D: `furniture.service_counter_presence_3d` (counter detected as an oriented planar surface with asymmetric standing/seated affordance on its two sides), `person.staff_role_position_3d` (staff figure positioned consistently on one side of the counter across multiple frames or viewpoints), `signage.service_role_text_presence_3d` (OCR over reconstructed signage planes), and `affordance.queue_path_3d` (continuous floor-marking or rail topology suggesting a single-file approach path). The VLM is invoked over a rendered viewpoint or a top-down floor projection; the binary output is unchanged from the 2D pathway, but extraction confidence is expected to be higher because the role-asymmetry is geometrically explicit rather than inferred from monocular cues.

### bn_notes
Sprint-2 candidate for ranking-theoretic recoding (Spohn, 2012, §5.3) — current binary CPT representation is a placeholder. The latent's epistemic content is naturally binary (the service script is readable or it is not), and a ranking function over (clear, unclear, undefined) better expresses the asymmetric belief revision that occurs when a visitor encounters a deliberately desk-less service encounter: the *absence* of the canonical sign-vehicle should not collapse the posterior to "unclear" but should instead trigger a higher-order check on cultural-frame priors. Sprint 2 should evaluate replacing the binary CPT with a rank-based encoding once the BN integration sprint begins; until then, the binary CPT is the lowest-overhead placeholder consistent with the registry schema.

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: Reading only `examples_positive` (clinic reception, hotel desk, restaurant host stand, bank queue, government triage, boutique counter, library reference desk) and `examples_negative` (omakase with no desk, unstaffed coworking desk, hotel with competing unlabelled counters, retail with no counter and no role markers, continental café with contradictory cue, billing-only desk with no entry signage), the construct that emerges is: *the joint legibility of role asymmetry and help-seeking locus in a service-encounter setting, where legibility depends on convergent multi-cue evidence rather than on the presence of any single artefact (most notably, not on the desk alone)*. The blind-reader pass also recovers the desk-as-category-mistake warning that lives in `scope_excludes`.

**Action taken**: none. The examples already discriminate the construct from its near-misses (desk-present-but-script-unclear; script-clear-but-desk-absent), and the blind-read recovers both the binary value type and the multi-cue convergence requirement.
