## Driver pass — semantics

### definition_short
Presenter-to-audience configuration: clear front, oriented seating, focal lighting on speaker.

### definition_long
A *one-to-many presentation* configuration is a focused gathering in which the spatial arrangement establishes an asymmetric performance frame: a single, clearly-marked "front region" oriented toward an aggregated audience whose seating, body axis, and gaze are co-aligned upon that front. The construct picks out the architectural and furniture-level commitments that *afford* this performance frame — a discernible focal zone (stage, podium, screen, board, instructor's desk), audience seating whose long axis points toward that focal zone, and (typically) a lighting or scale gradient that distinguishes presenter from audience. Following Goffman (1959, ch. 3), the configuration creates a front/back-region division with the audience as ratified recipients; following Goffman (1981, pp. 124–159), it fixes the participation framework into the asymmetric "platform format" footing in which one party speaks and the rest are positioned to listen. Kendon (1990, ch. 7) treats the same arrangement as the limiting case of a *highly asymmetric semicircular F-formation*, in which one of the participants holds the only "transmitting" position while the rest occupy receiving positions around an o-space whose centroid lies on the front-region boundary. L52 is binary on the presence/absence of this configuration: either the room's geometry commits to a one-to-many frame, or it does not.

### examples_positive (≥ 6)
1. A university lecture hall with tiered seating, every row oriented toward a podium and projection screen at the front; a lectern with focal lighting marks the presenter's region.
2. A conference breakout room with chairs arrayed in arcing rows facing a flip-chart easel and a small raised dais; the presenter's table is placed at the convergent focus of the seating.
3. A house of worship with pews oriented toward a pulpit and altar; congregational seating shares a single forward axis.
4. A school classroom with student desks in rows facing a whiteboard and the teacher's desk; the chalk tray and a wall-mounted projector mark the front region.
5. A small startup all-hands space: thirty stackable chairs arranged in a fan facing a single TV monitor and a standing presenter spot marked by a microphone stand.
6. A press-briefing room with a fixed podium under directional lighting, rows of chairs facing it, and a backdrop wall behind the podium that visually frames the speaker.
7. A wedding ceremony arrangement with chairs in two columns separated by an aisle, all oriented toward an officiant's position at one end framed by an arch.

### examples_negative (≥ 4)
1. A boardroom with twelve chairs around a single oblong table — every participant faces the table's centre; there is no front region and no asymmetric audience axis. (This is L48 vis-à-vis / L49 conference geometry, not L52.)
2. A cafe with small tables scattered across the floor, each table sociopetal in its own right, but with no shared focal front. (This is mingling/conversation geometry, not L52.)
3. A circular drum-circle arrangement of chairs with no privileged speaking position; participants face inward toward each other rather than outward toward a presenter. (Circular F-formation, not L52.)
4. A waiting room with chairs lining the perimeter walls, all oriented inward toward an empty central floor; there is no presenter region and no shared audience axis. (Sociofugal perimeter seating, not L52.)
5. An empty auditorium with seating fixed in rows but the front region completely undifferentiated — no podium, screen, lectern, or lighting marker — such that the geometry is ambiguous between a presentation hall and a holding area.

### scope_includes (≥ 3)
- Educational settings (classrooms, seminar rooms, lecture halls) where the front-region commitment is institutionally stable even when no presenter is present.
- Religious and civic-ceremony spaces (chapels, courtrooms, council chambers) whose seating asymmetry encodes a one-to-many participation frame.
- Outdoor or temporary configurations (a folding-chair audience facing a temporary stage) whose geometry commits to the platform format even without permanent architecture.

### scope_excludes (≥ 3)
- Symmetric F-formations (vis-à-vis, L-arrangement, circular) where no party occupies a privileged transmitting position — these are L44, L48, L49.
- Mingling configurations with no fixed seating and no front region — these are L56.
- Multi-focus arrangements where several small audience clusters each face their own front (e.g., a poster session, a science fair) — these are not one-to-many but many-to-many.
- Sociopetal perimeter seating around an empty centre, where the focal point is communal rather than performer-occupied (e.g., a campfire-style arrangement) — closer to L51 group assembly than to L52.

### extraction_notes_2d
A 2D image is sufficient when upstream observables jointly identify (a) a dedicated front region and (b) audience seating whose orientation axes converge on that front region. The extractor should consume upstream tags `affordance.podium_present`, `affordance.screen_or_board_present`, `affordance.lectern_present`, `furniture.chair_orientation_field` (a per-chair yaw vector), `furniture.seating_row_axis` (the principal axis of detected rows), and `lighting.focal_gradient_index` (whether one region of the scene shows a directional or higher-intensity lighting commitment than the rest). The binary positive judgement requires (i) at least one front-region marker AND (ii) ≥ 70% of detected audience-seating yaw vectors converging within ±30° of a single front direction. Symmetric F-formations (circular, vis-à-vis) are explicit negatives even if a screen is present, because the audience axis condition fails. The extractor should *also* emit the F-formation-geometry sub-fields required by Kendon-driven extractor contract: `(o_space_centroid_xy, o_space_radius_px, predominant_arrangement)` where for L52 `predominant_arrangement` will typically resolve to `"semicircular_asymmetric"` and the o-space centroid lies on the boundary between front region and audience. Region support is true: positive instances should return a polygon for the front region and a polygon for the audience seating block. Cross-cultural variance is medium: the platform format is robust across cultures (the asymmetric speaker-audience configuration recurs widely in pedagogical, ceremonial, and political settings), but cultures vary in *how strongly* the front-region marker is materially elaborated — a Japanese lecture hall may have minimal stage furniture relative to a Western analogue.

### extraction_notes_3d
In 3D / VR scans the same upstream observables are available with stronger geometric grounding: chair-orientation yaw vectors are recoverable from full mesh geometry rather than monocular pose estimation, and the focal-region commitment can be verified by computing whether the union of audience-chair viewing frusta intersects in a coherent region (the front region). The extractor should compute (i) per-chair viewing direction from chair-back normal vectors, (ii) the convergence point of those vectors (or the region of densest intersection if the vectors are near-parallel), (iii) whether a front-region marker (podium, screen, raised platform mesh) is present within that convergence region. A 3D positive instance requires the convergence region to coincide with at least one detected front-region marker. The F-formation o-space centroid is computed identically to the 2D case but with greater precision; the o-space radius can be computed from the mean distance from convergence point to first row of audience chairs. Monocular-3D-approximation extraction is also viable (Tier 1 in V2.6) using monocular depth estimation plus a planar-floor assumption; the binary judgement is robust to noise in depth estimation because it depends on the *direction* of seating axis convergence rather than absolute distances.

### bn_notes
L52 is one of three binary-candidate latents (with L46 and L53) for which the panel's Spohn voice flagged a Sprint-2 ranking-theoretic recoding recommendation. The binary `latent_score` value type is the right Sprint-1 commitment because (i) the configuration is genuinely categorical at first approximation — a room either commits to a one-to-many platform format or it does not — and (ii) the registry currently supports only probabilistic CPTs, and forcing a 5-state ordinal CPT for a near-binary construct would inflate the parameter count without explanatory gain. However, Spohn (2012, §5.3) argues that constructs of this binary "regime-switch" character are more naturally encoded as ranking functions over disbelief levels, which support default reasoning and graceful degradation under partial evidence. Sprint 2's BN-integration phase should evaluate whether L52 (and L46, L53) should be migrated from binary `latent_score` to a ranking-theoretic encoding once the registry schema admits ranking-function value types. Until then, the binary value type is honoured but should be regarded as provisional.

The Spohn 50%-overlap rule applies to `bn.parent_tags`: L52's parent set is dominated by `affordance.*` and `furniture.*` observables specific to presentation geometry, with `lighting.focal_gradient_index` providing an additional disjoint signal. The most likely overlap conflict in audit will be with L53 (audience attentional focus), which will share the `furniture.chair_orientation_field` parent. The pair is intentionally distinguished by L52's reliance on front-region affordance markers (podium, screen, lectern) versus L53's reliance on audience-side gaze and posture observables. If audit shows >50% overlap, the resolution is to remove `furniture.chair_orientation_field` from L53's parent set and let L53 depend on a more specific gaze-direction observable (e.g., `pose.head_yaw_field`) instead.

## Blind-reader pass — iteration log

### Iteration 1
*"Working only from the examples I wrote in Pass 1, can I reverse-engineer the construct?"*

**Inferred construct from examples alone**: Reading positives 1–7 only — lecture hall, conference breakout, house of worship, classroom, all-hands space, press-briefing room, wedding ceremony — all share three properties: (a) one architecturally distinguished spot (podium / pulpit / lectern / officiant position / TV monitor with mic stand), (b) seating that is *not* arranged symmetrically around a centre but rather *outward-asymmetric*, with a clearly-favoured forward direction, and (c) a tacit role asymmetry between the occupant of the distinguished spot and those in the seating. The negatives (boardroom, cafe, drum-circle, perimeter waiting room, undifferentiated auditorium) all violate at least one of these properties — usually the front-region commitment or the audience-axis convergence. Inferred construct: *a configuration in which architecture and furniture jointly commit the room to a one-to-many speaker-and-audience participation frame*.

**Comparison to Pass 1 definition**: Converged. The blind-read recovers the asymmetric platform-format commitment (Goffman 1981) and the front-region/audience-axis pair. The blind-read does not spontaneously surface the Kendon-as-asymmetric-semicircular-F-formation reading, but that is appropriate — that reading is a theoretical interpretation not visible in the examples themselves, and it belongs in the validation_notes as a preserved-disagreement record rather than in the construct's operational definition.

**Action taken**: None. Examples are sufficient to recover the construct. Negative example 5 (undifferentiated auditorium) is doing important work: it ensures the blind reader sees that *seating rows alone* are insufficient — the front-region commitment must be materially marked.

The Sprint 1 disposition (panel disposition §3 (xii)) rejected this folding for two reasons: (i) the V2.6 taxonomy has been shipped to students with L52 as a separate construct, and the registry's traceability to its source taxonomy outranks ontological purity within Sprint 1; (ii) folding L52 into the F-formation cluster mid-sprint would force a cascading re-key of all consumer contracts that already reference `social.presentation_one_to_many`. Kendon's argument is recorded here as one of the four explicit construct contests handed to the students for Week 5 deliberation (panel disposition §5, contest 3). Students should consider whether L52 should be folded into L48/L49/L44 as a categorical-value-of, retained as a separate latent with explicit cross-reference to the F-formation cluster, or split into a configuration-binary and an attentional-engagement pair (currently L52 + L53).
